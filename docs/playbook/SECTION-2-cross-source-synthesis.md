## 2A. Buyer Psychology — How Marketplace Buyers Actually Think

These are the consolidated, cross-validated truths about the P2P buyer. Each gives the insight, the supporting books, the product implication (what the copilot *does*), and the relevant buyer-intent IDs.

| # | Consolidated insight | Supported by | Product implication (copilot behavior) | INTENT ids |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **BP-1** | **The stated number is not the real interest.** "$20" hides a budget cap, a value-doubt, a deadline, or a comparison. Reconciling the interest beats splitting the position. | GTY, BFA, 3DN, GPN | Before any counter, ask one interest-probing question (PRIN-004); classify budget vs. value-doubt vs. timing vs. comparison, then pick the matching lever (bundle/proof/ship-fast). | INTENT-002, INTENT-003, INTENT-004 |
| **BP-2** | **Buyers decide emotionally (System 1), then rationalize.** Delivery and framing move the rationalization more than facts do. | NSD, INF, DC | Lead with tone/empathy, not data; label the emotion first (PRIN-013), then justify with comps (PRIN-003). | INTENT-004, INTENT-007 |
| **BP-3** | **Loss looms larger than gain.** "You might miss this" outpulls "you could have this." | NSD, INF, BFA, 3DN | At close, use *truthful* scarcity / loss framing (PRIN-011) — last one, real saves — never fabricated. | INTENT-005, INTENT-010 |
| **BP-4** | **Autonomy is sacred; pressure triggers reactance.** Cornering a buyer (must-decide-now) can flip desire into refusal. "No" feels safe and re-engages them. | INF, NSD, DC | Default to the freedom-restorer (PRIN-033); revive ghosters with a "No"-oriented question (PRIN-015), not a nag; one scarcity note max (COACH-014). | INTENT-005, INTENT-010, INTENT-007 |
| **BP-5** | **Buyers anchor on the first number/frame and adjust insufficiently** — even experts, even toward frames told to be meaningless. | BFA, 3DN, INF, NSD | List near the top of the defensible comp range (PRIN-009); let the buyer name a number first on "best price?" (PRIN-001); meta-frame the item up-market honestly (PRIN-035). | INTENT-002, INTENT-003 |
| **BP-6** | **Fairness is an emotional trigger and a weapon.** Buyers reject good-for-them deals that *feel* unfair, and weaponize "that's not fair" to extract concessions. | NSD, GTY, BFA | Depersonalize price with an external standard so it's not "you vs. them" (PRIN-003); offer a "what's your theory / what makes that fair?" probe. | INTENT-002, INTENT-003 |
| **BP-7** | **Buyers overestimate their own BATNA** ("I'll just find it cheaper") and feel the sum of vague alternatives, not the real one. | GTY, GPN, BFA, 3DN | Honestly shape their no-deal option — differentiate on condition/authenticity/ship speed (PRIN-011, PRIN-036); ask for the cheaper link rather than disparaging it. | INTENT-003 |
| **BP-8** | **Each micro-commitment (a like, a question, a "yeah") pulls the buyer toward the consistent next step.** | INF, BFA | Ladder small yeses → reserve → checkout (PRIN-032); treat the like as the first commitment; get the deal in writing (PRIN-025). | INTENT-001, INTENT-010 |
| **BP-9** | **Under uncertainty buyers defer to social proof and credible authority** — strongest from *similar* buyers and *genuine* proof. | INF, BFA, GTY, 3DN, DC | Surface true saves/sold-counts/reviews (PRIN-029) and real authenticity proof (PRIN-030); tailor "buyers like you" only when true. | INTENT-004, INTENT-012 |
| **BP-10** | **A buyer's aggression usually springs from fear** (of being scammed) and they **attribute intent from impact** ("I feel cheated → you cheated me"). | DC, GPN, NSD | Don't counter-punch; acknowledge the feeling first, set the intent-accusation aside, invite evidence (PRIN-041, PRIN-020). | INTENT-007, INTENT-012, INTENT-013 |
| **BP-11** | **Buyers cling to positions to save face**, especially after a public/committed stand, and are more convinced by reasons they discover themselves. | GPN, GTY, BFA | Build a golden bridge — a face-saving cover for the climb-down (PRIN-022, CONC-007); ask questions instead of lecturing. | INTENT-002, INTENT-005 |
| **BP-12** | **"Yes" is often counterfeit; a verbal yes with nothing to lose isn't a deal.** Real buy-in is "That's right" + a committed action. | NSD, BFA, INF | Drive to "That's right" with a summary (PRIN-016); close for commitment, on-platform, in writing (PRIN-025). | INTENT-001, INTENT-005 |
| **BP-13** | **Text strips tone, amplifying suspicion.** Terse/all-caps/no-emoji messages get read as hostile more readily than they are. | DC, NSD | On ambiguous terse messages, pause and send a neutral clarifier, don't assume hostility (PRIN-019, PRIN-012). | INTENT-014, INTENT-007 |
| **BP-14** | **Most buyers are cooperative, and many enjoy the haggle itself** (bragging rights); only a minority genuinely prefer no-haggle. | BFA, 3DN | Don't assume aggression is the norm; let the buyer "win" a labeled concession and the last round (PRIN-024, PRIN-028). | INTENT-001, INTENT-006, INTENT-011 |

## 2B. Seller Psychology — Casual-Seller Biases the Copilot Must Counter

The product's main user is a casual seller who systematically *leaves money on the table* and *over-reacts emotionally*. Each row: the bias/mistake, supporting books, what the copilot does about it, and the COACH ids.

| # | Seller mistake / bias | Supported by | Product implication (copilot counter) | COACH ids |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **SP-1** | **No floor / arbitrary bottom line.** Negotiates with no walk-away or a wishful number, causing both bad accepts and bad rejects. | GTY, GPN, BFA, 3DN | Compute a comp-based floor + "reconsider below $X" trip wire at listing time; prompt counter/hold/accept instead of one-tap accept (PRIN-010). | COACH-001 |
| **SP-2** | **Anchors on the floor, not the goal.** Relaxes the instant an offer clears the bottom line; buyers sense the relaxation. | BFA, 3DN | List near the top of the comp range; flag when a counter "leaks" toward the floor (PRIN-009). | COACH-002 |
| **SP-3** | **Chases "Yes" / over-accommodates to be nice.** Produces counterfeit yeses, ghosting, and exploitation by hard bargainers. | NSD, GTY, BFA | Detect over-accommodation in drafts; push a written confirmation + immediate on-platform offer to convert verbal yes → commitment (PRIN-025). | COACH-003 |
| **SP-4** | **Reflexively splits the difference** out of fear of confrontation — an arbitrary result that leaks margin. | NSD, 3DN, BFA, GTY | On "split it," propose an odd number / a term above the midpoint, gated by floor; allow a split only inside the comp range (PRIN-008, CONC-004). | COACH-004 |
| **SP-5** | **Blabs / goes first.** Names the lowest price when asked, over-explains, hands away leverage. | NSD, GTY, BFA, 3DN | Default to an interest-probe before any number; flag drafts that disclose floor/eagerness early (PRIN-001). | COACH-005 |
| **SP-6** | **"1-D" tunnel vision — slashes price when the listing is the problem.** | 3DN | "Why isn't this selling?" diagnostic scores photos/comps/bundle/framing/reply-latency *before* recommending a cut (PRIN-034). | COACH-006 |
| **SP-7** | **Fixed-pie bias.** Reads every objection as "cut the price," missing bundle/shipping/timing/proof fixes. | GTY, BFA, 3DN | Surface the likely non-price interest and propose hold-price-add-value or a bundle first (PRIN-005, PRIN-006). | COACH-007 |
| **SP-8** | **The reaction reflex.** Fires back at a rude/lowball message, or misreads a terse one and escalates irreversibly. | GPN, DC, INF, NSD | Cool-down/draft step on hostile inbound or angry drafts; offer a calmer reply or neutral clarifier (PRIN-019). | COACH-008 |
| **SP-9** | **Counter-punches rude buyers.** Tanks the deal, liking, and reputation. | NSD, GTY, GPN, BFA, INF, DC | Rewrite snippy drafts into purposeful ones; never generate a counter-attack (PRIN-002, PRIN-020). | COACH-009 |
| **SP-10** | **Vents / hit-and-run.** Sends a satisfying jab that earns a worse review and a dispute. | DC | Block/reshape furious drafts ("Maybe read the description next time") or advise holding (no clear constructive purpose → don't send). | COACH-010 |
| **SP-11** | **Threatens instead of warning; yanks the offer in anger.** | GPN, 3DN, BFA | Frame relist/other-interest as a neutral fact and always re-state the standing offer in re-engagement (PRIN-023). | COACH-011 |
| **SP-12** | **Squeezes the last dollar / gloats at close.** Risks reneging, bad feedback, no repeat business. | GPN, GTY, INF | Bias the closing message toward a tiny final courtesy; track which closers correlate with reviews/repeat buyers (PRIN-024). | COACH-012 |
| **SP-13** | **Temptation to fake the trigger.** Invents saves, sold counts, phantom buyers, comps, authentication "because it works." | INF, GTY, BFA, 3DN | Hard guardrail: every claim about demand/supply/authenticity/comparison is checked against real data before sending (PRIN-011, PRIN-029). | COACH-013 |
| **SP-14** | **Over-pressures.** Repeated nudges / fake deadlines trigger reactance and kill the sale. | INF, DC | Limit to one factual scarcity note; default to the freedom-restorer; suppress repeated auto-nudges (PRIN-033). | COACH-014 |
| **SP-15** | **Jumps to the fix on complaints**, skipping acknowledgment; reads as "I don't care." | DC, GPN, NSD | Draft complaint replies that lead with specific, genuine acknowledgment before options (skip on calm messages) (PRIN-020). | COACH-015 |
| **SP-16** | **Defends good intentions when accused** ("I'd never scam you"), making the buyer angrier. | DC, NSD, GTY | On "scammer/fake," draft impact-not-intent: acknowledge feeling → set intent aside → invite proof (PRIN-041). | COACH-016 |
| **SP-17** | **Blame frame / won't own real fault** (vague listing, slow ship, missed flaw). | DC | When the seller clearly erred, draft clean specific ownership + fair options; guardrail against misapplying "we both contributed" to a defrauded buyer (PRIN-042). | COACH-017 |
| **SP-18** | **Tries to control the buyer's reaction** → over-concedes "to avoid the hassle," training extortion. | DC, BFA | In hostile/extortion threads, coach holding a fair documented offer and letting the platform process protect them (PRIN-040). | COACH-018 |
| **SP-19** | **Tolerates dirty tricks silently** (demand creep, phantom offers) or retaliates. | GTY, GPN, BFA | Detect nibbles/demand-creep; draft "that's a new deal, here are the agreed terms" + a reciprocity test; never coach using these tricks. | COACH-019 |
| **SP-20** | **Style blind spots.** Accommodators/compromisers cave fast; competers bruise relationships; avoiders never ask. | BFA, DC | Infer the user's style over time; tailor nudges to the gap (PRIN-045). | COACH-020 |
| **SP-21** | **Treats deadlines as the enemy; panic-markdowns.** | NSD, 3DN | Recommend relist timing and a measured markdown ladder (−10% / 7–10 days); discourage reactive deep cuts (PRIN-038). | COACH-021 |
| **SP-22** | **Damages own walk-away** — open-ended unpaid "holds," serial (not parallel) buyers. | 3DN, GTY | Counsel against unpaid holds (deposit / first-to-checkout); respond to all interested buyers, accept first firm commitment (PRIN-025). | COACH-022 |
| **SP-23** | **Leaves the social contract unspoken** — vague condition/ship/return expectations → disputes & bad reviews. | 3DN, DC | Listing generator proactively states condition (with flaw photos), dispatch time, return policy (PRIN-037). | COACH-023 |
| **SP-24** | **Identity threat** — one complaint feels like "I'm a bad seller," tipping into denial or panic-refund. | DC | Reframe a single complaint as normal/solvable; steer to the two-option fair resolution (PRIN-043). | COACH-024 |

**Cross-cutting pattern.** Seller mistakes cluster into two families the copilot treats differently: **value-leakage** (SP-1–7, 21–22 — under-asking, caving, fixed-pie, bad timing), countered by *coaching nudges that add friction before a concession*; and **emotional self-sabotage** (SP-8–18, 24 — reacting, venting, panic-refunding, people-pleasing), countered by *cool-down/draft guardrails that add friction before a send*. The single most important guardrail across both is COACH-013 (never fake the trigger), because it is the only one that protects the seller from *themselves and the platform simultaneously*.

## 2C. Negotiation Principles — Ranked Master List (Top 20 for P2P)

The full canonical table is the **principles asset (PRIN-001 … PRIN-045)**; do not re-derive all 45 here. Below are the ~20 that matter most for casual P2P resale, expanded. Each: meaning · why · when · when-not · example situation · example buyer message · recommended response · citations. Ordered by marketplace-fit and pipeline position.

> **Format note:** "Citations" lists supporting books then the primary source in **bold**.

### PRIN-001 — Bounce the price back (let the buyer name a number first)
- **Meaning:** Answer "what's your lowest?" with an open question that makes the buyer reveal their budget; never volunteer a discount.
- **Why:** Their first number is usually above your floor; disclosing first leaks leverage. Probing buys information = leverage.
- **When:** Any "best price?" / "lowest you'll go?" opener before a number is on the table.
- **When not:** You want a fast fixed-price sale to an informed pro; trivial items where the dance costs more than the gap.
- **Situation:** Buyer DMs a one-line price probe with no offer.
- **Buyer:** "What's the lowest you'll go?" · **Response:** "I'm open to a fair offer — what were you hoping to spend?"
- **Citations:** NSD, GTY, BFA, 3DN — primary **NSD**.

### PRIN-002 — Calibrated / problem-solving question (deflect the demand into their problem)
- **Meaning:** Answer a lowball/demand with a deferential "How/What" question that makes the buyer solve your constraint, not a flat "no."
- **Why:** Creates forced empathy and the illusion of control; they talk themselves up or withdraw without a fight. (Merges Voss "How am I supposed to do that?", Ury open question, GTY "What's your theory?", Shell probe-first.)
- **When:** Direct lowballs; "free shipping or no deal"; "knock off $X."
- **When not:** Aggressive tone turns it into an accusation; pair with a fact for data-buyers; just accept a clean fair offer.
- **Situation:** The #1 lowball case on eBay/Posh/Depop.
- **Buyer:** "I'll give you $20." · **Response:** "I appreciate the offer — how am I supposed to do $20 when these sell for $45? What were you hoping to spend?"
- **Citations:** NSD, GTY, GPN, BFA — primary **NSD**.

### PRIN-003 — Justify the price with sold comps (objective criteria / normative leverage)
- **Meaning:** Defend the number with an external standard (recent solds, condition norms), reasoning first, figure second.
- **Why:** An agreement grounded in a standard is harder to attack and depersonalizes price — conceding to the standard isn't conceding to *you*. (Merges GTY objective criteria, Shell normative leverage, 3DN "because," Ury "what makes that fair.")
- **When:** Any price challenge, lowball, or "competitor cheaper"; defending a firm price.
- **When not:** No real comp exists (never fabricate — pivot to interests/options); when you're genuinely above market the standard cuts against you.
- **Situation:** Buyer challenges the asking price as unfair.
- **Buyer:** "$45 is too much, nobody pays that." · **Response:** "Fair to push on price — the last three sold for $48–55 in this condition (happy to screenshot), so $45 is already under that. If you've seen one cheaper, send the link and I'll take a real look."
- **Citations:** GTY, BFA, 3DN, GPN, NSD — primary **GTY**.

### PRIN-004 — Decode the interest behind the position
- **Meaning:** The number hides a real interest (budget cap, value-doubt, occasion, comparison); satisfy the interest without dropping price.
- **Why:** For every interest there are several satisfying positions; reconciling interests produces wiser deals and reveals cheap non-price fixes.
- **When:** Every lowball or stall before you counter; a liked item that won't convert.
- **When not:** Interest is obvious and met; interrogating a fast clean sale.
- **Situation:** Lowball with no stated reason.
- **Buyer:** "Would you take $20? (listed $45)" · **Response:** "Happy to work with you — what were you hoping to spend, and is this for something specific? Helps me see what I can do beyond the number."
- **Citations:** GTY, BFA, 3DN, GPN — primary **GTY**.

### PRIN-005 — Hold price, add value (trade non-monetary terms / dovetail)
- **Meaning:** Keep the price, trade things cheap to you but valuable to them: absorbed shipping, a throw-in, a held item, faster dispatch.
- **Why:** A price cut costs dollar-for-dollar; an option can be near-free to you, high-value to them.
- **When:** Gap between your floor and their ceiling; bundle interest; "I want a deal."
- **When not:** Single cheap item where complexity annoys; buyer only wants the one item cheaper.
- **Situation:** Small price gap on a single item.
- **Buyer:** "Can you do $25 instead of $30?" · **Response:** "I'd rather keep it at $30 since it's priced fairly — but I'll cover shipping, which works out about the same for you. Deal?"
- **Citations:** NSD, GTY, BFA, 3DN — primary **3DN**.

### PRIN-006 — Bundle to grow the pie (move northeast)
- **Meaning:** Turn a one-item haggle into a larger sale: pair the wanted item with related stock at a small blended discount + combined shipping.
- **Why:** A bigger pie means each share can be bigger; moves more inventory at better blended margin and gives the buyer a face-saving lower per-item price.
- **When:** Buyer is haggling one item and you have related stock; closet clear-outs; multiple likes.
- **When not:** Buyer wants exactly one item with no use for anything else.
- **Situation:** Buyer lowballs one tee; has liked another.
- **Buyer:** "Would you take $18 on the green tee?" · **Response:** "You've also liked the blue one — take both for $32 instead of $40 and I'll cover shipping. More for you, one package for me."
- **Citations:** GTY, BFA, 3DN, INF — primary **3DN**.

### PRIN-007 — Shrinking-increment concession ladder, never for free
- **Meaning:** Concede in steps that each get smaller, slow near the floor, end on an odd number, and attach a condition/reason to every move.
- **Why:** Decreasing increments signal you're near your limit; reciprocity makes the buyer counter; free/escalating concessions teach them to keep pushing. (Merges Voss Ackerman, Shell shrinking ladder + If/then, 3DN converging concessions, Cialdini rejection-then-retreat.)
- **When:** Multi-message haggles with a committed buyer; higher-value items.
- **When not:** Low-value quick flips; first lowball to rule out; strong BATNA means don't move.
- **Situation:** Multi-round haggle, list $50 / floor $42.
- **Buyer:** "$35… $40?… $42?" · **Response:** "I came down to $44 already — I can do $43 if we close now, and that's really as far as I can go."
- **Citations:** NSD, BFA, 3DN, INF — primary **NSD**. *(See Conflict §C-1.)*

### PRIN-008 — Don't reflexively split the difference (no deal beats a bad deal)
- **Meaning:** Refuse the automatic "meet me halfway"; counter with a small odd move or a term anchored to your floor, ready to relist.
- **Why:** The midpoint of two arbitrary numbers is itself arbitrary; reciprocity already drags outcomes there. Readiness to walk removes neediness = leverage.
- **When:** Buyer pushes "just split it"; thin-margin items; serial discounters.
- **When not:** Item is aging, you need cash/space, and halfway still clears your floor.
- **Situation:** Buyer proposes the midpoint of two anchors.
- **Buyer:** "You're at $40, I'm at $30, let's just do $35." · **Response:** "I get the instinct to split it, but $35 doesn't quite work. I can do $38 today, otherwise no worries — I'm happy to hold out."
- **Citations:** NSD, 3DN, BFA — primary **NSD**. *(Direct conflict with GTY — see §C-1.)*

### PRIN-009 — Anchor on your goal, not your floor (high-but-defensible list price)
- **Meaning:** Set the listing and counters at the top of the defensible fair range; keep the walk-away floor out of focus.
- **Why:** People who expect more get more; floor-anchoring makes you stop striving and buyers sense it; the first number sets the zone.
- **When:** Setting list price; first counter; whenever you have leverage and a comp.
- **When not:** No leverage and the buyer knows it; aging unsold item; never an anchor that fails the giggle test.
- **Situation:** Buyer asks for the lowest right after listing.
- **Buyer:** "What's the lowest you'll go?" · **Response:** "Based on recent solds ($48–55), $50 is already a good deal — that's where I'm at. Happy to bundle if you want more value."
- **Citations:** BFA, 3DN, INF, GPN — primary **BFA**. *(See Conflict §C-2.)*

### PRIN-010 — Know your BATNA: relist/hold is your walk-away (and trip wire)
- **Meaning:** Decide before negotiating what you'll do if it doesn't sell; judge every offer against that, with a "reconsider below $X" trip wire, not a rigid bottom line.
- **Why:** A BATNA is a smarter measure than a guessed floor; the better it is, the more power you have. A trip wire blocks heat-of-the-moment regret-accepts.
- **When:** Setting accept/counter/decline thresholds; any "final offer" moment.
- **When not:** As a static number — recompute as saves/watchers/days-listed change.
- **Situation:** A take-it-or-leave-it at your reconsider line.
- **Buyer:** "$30 take it or leave it." · **Response:** "I appreciate the straight offer. At $30 I'd rather relist — it's getting steady interest at $45. I could meet you at $40 today, though, and we're done. Deal?"
- **Citations:** GTY, GPN, BFA, 3DN — primary **GTY**.

### PRIN-011 — Shape the buyer's no-deal option with truthful scarcity
- **Meaning:** Make the buyer aware — *only when true* — that their alternative is worse: last one, real saves/watchers, not restocking, price rising.
- **Why:** Outcomes improve when the buyer sees their walk-away as bad and getting worse; scarcity raises perceived value; loss aversion pushes Act-Now.
- **When:** Hesitating buyer at close with genuine competing interest or a real constraint.
- **When not:** **GUARDRAIL** — never fabricate buyers, countdowns, or relist dates. Mass-available items.
- **Situation:** Fence-sitter says "maybe next payday."
- **Buyer:** "Let me think about it." · **Response:** "No pressure at all! Just so you're not disappointed — it's the only one I have and a couple of people have it saved. Here whenever you're ready."
- **Citations:** NSD, GTY, BFA, 3DN, INF — primary **INF**.

### PRIN-013 — Label the emotion to defuse it ("It seems like…")
- **Meaning:** Name the buyer's likely feeling as a neutral statement (never starting with "I"), then let it sit.
- **Why:** Labeling shifts brain activity from the amygdala to rational areas — a shortcut to feeling understood. (Merges Voss labeling + DC acknowledge-feelings.)
- **When:** Buyer is hesitant, frustrated, suspicious, stalling — clear the emotion before price.
- **When not:** Calm transactional messages (patronizing); don't manufacture emotion or stack a question right after.
- **Situation:** Hesitant value-doubt.
- **Buyer:** "Idk, $40 feels like a lot for something secondhand." · **Response:** "It sounds like you want to be sure you're getting real value before you commit — totally fair."
- **Citations:** NSD, DC, GPN — primary **NSD**.

### PRIN-015 — "No"-oriented question to re-engage a ghosting buyer
- **Meaning:** Revive a silent buyer with one short question that invites "No" and implies you'll move on.
- **Why:** "No" reaffirms autonomy and lowers guard; loss aversion makes them reply to reclaim control rather than be abandoned.
- **When:** Buyer who offered or asked about availability then went silent 24h+.
- **When not:** More than once per buyer (nagging); one who already clearly declined.
- **Situation:** Silence after an availability ping.
- **Buyer:** *(silence)* · **Response:** "Have you given up on this jacket? Totally fine if it's no longer right — just want to know whether to keep holding it for you."
- **Citations:** NSD, GPN, DC — primary **NSD**.

### PRIN-018 — Reasoning before the number; make the close one easy "yes"
- **Meaning:** Lead with the standard/reason, then the figure as "one fair option," shaped as a single yes/no.
- **Why:** Lead with a number and the buyer stops listening and starts countering; lead with reasoning and they follow you to it.
- **When:** Sending a counter or final price; closing a long haggle.
- **When not:** Over-explaining a trivial price; burying a simple accept under a paragraph.
- **Situation:** Buyer asks for "your actual best."
- **Buyer:** "So what's your actual best?" · **Response:** "Given the condition and that comparable ones sell for $48–55, here's a fair one: $44 shipped, and I'll toss in the dust bag. That work?"
- **Citations:** GTY, BFA, 3DN — primary **GTY**.

### PRIN-019 — Go to the balcony (pause before replying to a triggering message)
- **Meaning:** When provoked, suspend the instinct to strike back/cave/break off; draft, re-read calm, then send.
- **Why:** The first casualty of an attack is your objectivity; text strips tone so your worst-case read is as likely wrong as right.
- **When:** The instant an insult, derisive lowball, or terse ambiguous message lands.
- **When not:** Genuinely time-sensitive logistics; don't let the pause become ghosting.
- **Situation:** Hostile lowball.
- **Buyer:** "$10 take it or leave it, this isn't worth what you're asking lol" · **Response:** "Thanks for the offer! Let me take a look at where I can land on price and get back to you in a sec."
- **Citations:** GPN, DC, INF, NSD — primary **GPN**.

### PRIN-020 — Step to their side (acknowledge before you counter)
- **Meaning:** Let the buyer feel heard first — paraphrase the concern, validate it as reasonable — before defending price or saying no.
- **Why:** Listening may be the cheapest concession you can make; a heard buyer becomes less reactive and far more willing to hear your terms.
- **When:** Buyer venting, frustrated, or piling complaints into one message.
- **When not:** Clean offer you'll accept; don't manufacture a "tell me more" detour.
- **Situation:** Frustrated price + photo complaint.
- **Buyer:** "This is overpriced. Secondhand stuff shouldn't cost this much and the photos are dark." · **Response:** "I hear you — you want to be sure it's worth it and that you can actually see what you're getting. Totally reasonable. Let me add brighter photos and walk you through the price."
- **Citations:** GPN, DC, NSD, GTY — primary **GPN**.

### PRIN-022 — Build a golden bridge (give a face-saving way to climb down)
- **Meaning:** Don't push a stuck buyer toward your price; frame their move as a win (bundle, "prices just dropped," new-buyer deal) so retreating looks like an advance.
- **Why:** Resistance often comes from fear of losing face after a public stand; pushing widens the gap, a bridge reframes the retreat as a victory.
- **When:** Buyer close but stuck after a hard public line (e.g., commented "$25 max").
- **When not:** No real interest to meet; **GUARDRAIL** — the cover must be true, not a fabricated sale.
- **Situation:** Buyer locked to a stated max.
- **Buyer:** "I told you $25 is my max." · **Response:** "Tell you what — I'm running a bundle deal this week, so if you add the matching item I can do the pair for $40, which gets this one near your number. Want me to set that up?"
- **Citations:** GPN, INF, BFA — primary **GPN**.

### PRIN-024 — Be generous at the end (close for mutual satisfaction)
- **Meaning:** When closing after a haggle, concede a tiny final crumb (free shipping, fast dispatch, a sticker) and let the buyer win the last round.
- **Why:** A crushed buyer may renege, leave bad feedback, or never return; a satisfied one completes, reviews well, and returns.
- **When:** Closing any deal, especially after a tense haggle.
- **When not:** Don't confuse generosity with exploitation — the last crumb, not your margin; don't re-drop after "final."
- **Situation:** Buyer accepts the haggled price.
- **Buyer:** "Ok I'll take it at $38. Can you ship today?" · **Response:** "Done — $38 it is, and yes I'll get it out today. I think you got a great deal on this one. I'll send tracking when it's on its way!"
- **Citations:** GPN, GTY, INF — primary **GPN**.

### PRIN-025 — Close for commitment, not just agreement (in writing, on-platform)
- **Meaning:** Turn "I'll take it" into an action that makes backing out costly: confirm terms in the thread, send the offer/invoice immediately, keep it on-platform.
- **Why:** A "yes" isn't a deal until the buyer has something to lose; written/active commitments bind harder and cut ghosting.
- **When:** The moment a buyer agrees to a price.
- **When not:** Heavy commitment devices on a tiny trusted sale; don't shame a hesitant buyer.
- **Situation:** Verbal agreement reached.
- **Buyer:** "Ok yeah let's do $45." · **Response:** "Perfect — confirming $45 plus $5 shipping, total $50. I've just sent the offer; accept it whenever and I'll pack and ship today."
- **Citations:** BFA, INF, NSD — primary **BFA**.

### PRIN-026 — Detect the buyer's bargaining style and match your move
- **Meaning:** Read the opener for style (competing / collaborating / compromising / accommodating / avoiding) and pick a matched approach.
- **Why:** The right move against a competitive flipper (firm + leverage) is wrong against an accommodating sentimental buyer (relationship + generosity).
- **When:** Every thread at first contact — the opener is the richest signal.
- **When not:** Don't over-classify on one ambiguous line; hold the label loosely.
- **Situation:** Warm, deferential discount request.
- **Buyer:** "Love this! Would you maybe consider a small discount? Totally fine if not :)" · **Response:** "Thank you, that's so kind! I can do a little something since you clearly love it — $X works and I'll pack it really carefully for you."
- **Citations:** BFA, 3DN, INF — primary **BFA**.

### PRIN-030 — Answer trust/authenticity with genuine proof (authority)
- **Meaning:** Meet authenticity/condition doubts with real evidence — receipts, serials, stitching close-ups, demonstrated specifics — not bare reassurance.
- **Why:** Buyers defer to credible authority and proof; for a stranger online, demonstrated knowledge directly answers the trust objection.
- **When:** Buyer questions authenticity/condition/competence and you have the proof.
- **When not:** **GUARDRAIL** — never claim expertise/authentication you don't have.
- **Situation:** Authenticity skeptic.
- **Buyer:** "How do I know this bag is real?" · **Response:** "Great question — I've added photos of the date code (inside the side pocket) and the original receipt. The hardware's engraved, not stamped — one of the real-vs-fake tells. Happy to photograph any detail you want."
- **Citations:** INF, GTY, 3DN, DC — primary **INF**.

### PRIN-033 — Restore the buyer's freedom to defuse reactance
- **Meaning:** State true scarcity once, then explicitly hand control back ("no pressure, it's here whenever"); avoid repeated nudges and countdowns.
- **Why:** Threatened freedom makes people want the thing less and walk; giving freedom often closes better. This is the *safety-default tone*.
- **When:** Hesitant or independent buyer; whenever tempted to push hard; "stop messaging me."
- **When not:** Never replace with manufactured can't-come-back pressure.
- **Situation:** Buyer bristles at pressure.
- **Buyer:** "Stop messaging me, I'm still thinking." · **Response:** "Of course — no pressure at all, take your time. It's here whenever you're ready and I'm happy to answer anything."
- **Citations:** INF, DC — primary **INF**.

### PRIN-044 — Refuse off-platform payment on principle, not suspicion (safety override)
- **Meaning:** Decline off-platform pay / "pay outside the app" warmly with an "I feel safer keeping it on the app, protects us both" frame; never negotiate a scam signal.
- **Why:** On-platform keeps buyer/seller protection and gives both sides something to lose; framing it as mutual protection holds the line without insult. **A hard safety stop, not a lever.**
- **When:** "Venmo F&F," "pay this link," "ship first and I'll pay," code-sharing, overpayment setups.
- **When not:** Never relax it; balcony/bridge logic does NOT apply — hard-stop and, if pressed, disengage/report.
- **Situation:** Off-platform payment push.
- **Buyer:** "Just send it to my address and I'll Venmo you outside the app, trust me." · **Response:** "I appreciate you wanting to make it easy! I feel more comfortable keeping it on the app though — it protects us both if anything goes sideways. Happy to go ahead at the agreed price right here whenever you're ready."
- **Citations:** All seven — primary **DC**. *(Synthesized/inferred: the negotiation framing is sourced; the scam taxonomy is marketplace data, not in the books.)*

### PRIN-045 — Coach the seller around their own style's blind spot
- **Meaning:** Counter the user's bargaining-style weakness: nudge accommodators/compromisers to hold a round and ask before caving; remind competers to warm up with repeat buyers; prompt avoiders to make the ask.
- **Why:** Each style has a failure mode; coaching to the personal gap is where the copilot adds the most seller-side value.
- **When:** Continuously; especially when the user is about to under-ask or over-cave.
- **When not:** Don't override a deliberate, situation-appropriate choice.
- **Situation:** User about to over-concede on a first lowball.
- **Buyer:** *(user about to send "ok fine $25 is fine" — listed $40, first offer $20)* · **Response (to seller):** "Before you send that — you're going from $40 to $25 on their first lowball, which signals lots of room. Try: 'I can do $35 if you grab it today.' You can always come down again."
- **Citations:** BFA, DC — primary **BFA**.

### Key Principle Conflicts and Reconciliations

**§C-1 — Split the difference: Voss vs. Fisher (the central conflict).**
NSD (PRIN-008) says *never* split the difference — "one black and one brown shoe" — the midpoint of two arbitrary numbers is arbitrary. GTY (PRIN-003) says a split *between two figures each backed by a real standard* is fair and legitimate. **Reconciliation (encoded in PRIN-007/008 and CONC-004):** never split two *arbitrary* anchors, but a split that lands *inside the comp-supported fair range and above your floor* is acceptable. Operationally the copilot gates every midpoint behind (a) the seller's floor/trip wire (PRIN-010) and (b) a real standard (PRIN-003); if both pass, "split" is allowed and framed as final; otherwise it offers an odd number or a term above the midpoint.

**§C-2 — Anchor high vs. anchor on BATNA.**
Shell/3DN/Cialdini (PRIN-009) push a high, contrast-setting anchor to claim value. GTY prefers a BATNA-based reservation over an arbitrary anchor and warns that aggressive anchors damage relationships. **Reconciliation:** anchor high *to claim value*, but the anchor must be comp-defensible (PRIN-003) and pass the "giggle test," while the actual accept/counter/decline decision is governed by BATNA (PRIN-010). Anchoring sets the *opening*; BATNA governs the *exit*. They operate at different moments and don't truly collide once sequenced.

**§C-3 — Persuasion levers vs. anti-manipulation ethics.**
Cialdini's six levers (scarcity, social proof, authority, reciprocity, commitment, liking) each have a manipulative twin; the entire DC/Shell posture (honesty, "tell the truth slowly," learning stance) pulls the other way. **Reconciliation:** Cialdini's *own* rule resolves it — the genuine version works as well as the fake, so the copilot never needs to fabricate a trigger. COACH-013 hard-blocks any claim about demand/supply/authenticity/comparison that can't be checked against real data. Influence supplies the *levers*; Difficult Conversations and Shell supply the *governor*.

**§C-4 — Firmness vs. warmth (relationship tension).**
NSD/BFA reward firmness and leverage; DC/GPN/INF reward warmth, acknowledgment, and freedom-restoring. **Reconciliation (PRIN-027, Situational Matrix):** firmness is dialed by relationship × stakes. One-off stranger + high stakes → firm + anchor (BFA). Repeat/local buyer → accommodate (BFA/INF). Hostile-but-recoverable → balcony + bridge (GPN). Genuine bully/extortion → leverage + safety report (PRIN-040). The "And Stance" (DC) is the everyday default that holds both at once: warm to the person, firm on the number.

(fabricated) | 3DN, INF, BFA |

| **Face-saving cover** | Let a buyer climb down gracefully | "Since you're grabbing two…" · "Prices just dropped, so…" · "New-buyer deal." | Erodes trust if transparently fake | "I just started a bundle deal, so the pair's $40." | "Fine, I'll give you the discount you keep demanding." | GPN, INF |
| **Freedom-restorer** | Defuse reactance (the safety default) | "No pressure." · "It's here whenever you're ready." | None — this is the default | "No rush, take your time." | "Last chance, decide RIGHT NOW." | INF, DC |
| **Two-option close** | Give agency while protecting your interest | "Two options: `{A}` or `{B}`. Which works better?" | Offering options before acknowledging feelings | "Full refund on return, or keep it and I refund 25% now — your call." | "I'll do whatever, just stop." (over-concession) | DC |
| **Generous close** | Lock the sale and seed goodwill | "I think you got a great deal." · "I'll ship today." · "Thanks so much!" | Over-giving margin to please | "Done at $38, shipping today — great deal, thank you!" | "Fine. $38. Whatever." | GPN, INF |
| **Use your name** | Humanize for forced empathy | "Hey, I'm `{name}`!" | Forced in a one-line offer exchange | "I'm Sam, thanks for the interest!" | (n/a — low risk) | NSD |
| **Odd-number close** | Signal a final, calculated limit | "`{$42.50}` is my limit." | Silly precision on tiny items | "$43 vs $45." | "$43.27 on a $5 item." | NSD |

**Cross-cutting tactic rules the copilot enforces:**
1. **Question + silence** beats a wall of text — ask one open question, then stop typing (GTY, BFA). Looks like ghosting only if overused.
2. **Strip "but," insert "and"; convert "you are" → "I see/feel"** as a formatting pass on every outgoing reply (GPN, DC).
3. **Acknowledge before you counter** — feeling/concern in sentence one, position in sentence two (GPN, DC, NSD).
4. **Every scarcity/social-proof/comp phrase is truth-gated** before it can render (COACH-013) — this is the line between Cialdini-honest and Cialdini-manipulative.

## 2E. Objection-Handling Taxonomy

The full reply library (warm / firm / data-backed variations per objection, with escalate-or-disengage conditions) is the **objection-library asset (OBJ-xxx)**. Here is the taxonomy, how the classes relate, and the decision logic for signal strength.

### The 11 objection classes (OBJ-xxx)

| Class | What the buyer is really doing | Core principles |
|---|---|---|
| **OBJ-is-it-available** | A low-signal buying ping needing a nudge | PRIN-039, PRIN-032 |
| **OBJ-price-too-high** | Value-doubt or fairness signal, rarely a flat refusal | PRIN-020, PRIN-003, PRIN-009 |
| **OBJ-lowball** | Budget cap, habitual opener, or floor-probe | PRIN-002, PRIN-004, PRIN-007, PRIN-008 |
| **OBJ-competitor-cheaper** | Reassurance-seeking; often an inflated/bluffed alternative | PRIN-003, PRIN-011, PRIN-036 |
| **OBJ-condition-doubt** | Value-doubt / fear of disappointment | PRIN-013, PRIN-030, PRIN-036 |
| **OBJ-shipping-cost** | Total feels high / checkout surprise | PRIN-005, PRIN-006 |
| **OBJ-wants-bundle-deal** | Wants overall value — the best integrative opening | PRIN-006, PRIN-005, PRIN-022 |
| **OBJ-stalling-ghosting** | An unmet blocker or inertia; possibly an avoider | PRIN-015, PRIN-023, PRIN-033, PRIN-025 |
| **OBJ-trust-authenticity** | Security fear; aggression here springs from fear | PRIN-030, PRIN-029, PRIN-041 |
| **OBJ-rude-aggressive** | Fear, a rattle-tactic, or a bully/extortion move | PRIN-019, PRIN-020, PRIN-040 |
| **OBJ-off-platform-request** | Convenience — or, more often, a scam to strip protection | PRIN-044 (safety stop) |

### How the classes relate

The eleven objections aren't a flat list; they form a small map along two axes — **pre-sale vs. post-sale**, and **price-rooted vs. trust-rooted vs. safety**.

- **Price-rooted cluster** (price-too-high, lowball, competitor-cheaper, shipping-cost, wants-bundle): all are surface expressions of the same underlying question — *"is this fair / can I do better?"* They share a decision spine: **don't react to the number → decode the interest (PRIN-004) → justify on comps (PRIN-003) → hold price and trade value (PRIN-005/006) before conceding on a shrinking ladder (PRIN-007).** Lowball and competitor-cheaper are the same objection at different volumes; bundle and shipping are the *constructive* members (they hand you a value-trade rather than demanding a cut).
- **Trust-rooted cluster** (condition-doubt, trust-authenticity): both are *value-doubt*, not price — the fix is **proof and contingent terms (PRIN-030, PRIN-036), never a discount.** A common copilot error to prevent: dropping price when proof would have closed it. Post-sale, both flow into the dispute arc (acknowledge → own contribution → two options).
- **The two outliers** sit at the edges. **is-it-available** is the weakest pre-sale signal (a ping). **off-platform-request** is not really an objection at all — it's a **safety stop** that exits the negotiation machine entirely.
- **rude-aggressive** is a *cross-cutting modifier*: any price- or trust-rooted objection can arrive wrapped in hostility, which routes it first through the balcony (PRIN-019) and the classifier's bully check before the underlying objection is handled.

A natural escalation chain runs **lowball → competitor-cheaper → price-too-high → rude-aggressive → bully/extortion**, where each step is the previous one with more pressure; the copilot's job is to stop the slide by acknowledging early and refusing to reward escalation.

### Decision logic: high-signal vs. low-signal

The copilot grades each objection on **buying intent** to decide how much effort to invest and how hard to push.

**High-signal (lean in, invest, drive toward commitment):**
- **wants-bundle-deal** — highest-value: the buyer is *expanding* the deal. Move northeast (PRIN-006).
- **condition-doubt / trust-authenticity** — the buyer wants to buy but needs confidence; proof converts them (PRIN-030). High intent masked as worry.
- **price-too-high / reasonable offer** — engaged and negotiating in good faith; justify and trade (PRIN-003/005).
- **shipping-cost** — almost-closed; a cheap absorb usually lands it (PRIN-005).

**Low-signal (convert gently, don't over-invest, don't pressure):**
- **is-it-available** — a ping; one warm forward-motion step, never a hard pitch (PRIN-039).
- **stalling-ghosting** — inertia or an unmet blocker; one "No"-oriented revival + freedom-restorer, then stop (PRIN-015, PRIN-033). Over-nudging here triggers reactance (BP-4).
- **lowball with no engagement** — often a non-serious bargain-hunter; decode once, decline-with-reason, hold the standing offer; don't pour effort in (CONC-001).

**Negative-signal (protect, don't sell):**
- **off-platform-request** with scam markers (ship-first, overpayment, unverifiable screenshot) → **hard stop, do not ship, report** (PRIN-044, INTENT-009). Never apply balcony/bridge logic.
- **rude-aggressive that becomes extortion** ("discount or 1-star") → respond on leverage, offer at most the fair original terms, document, report (PRIN-040, CONC-010). A concession here is *negative* expected value: it rewards and trains the behavior.

**The governing rule:** effort and pressure scale with intent, but *pressure inverts at the top*. The hottest leads (bundlers, value-doubters) get effort but also the freedom-restorer, because pushing a buyer who's already leaning in triggers reactance (BP-4). The copilot pushes hardest in the *middle* of the intent spectrum and eases off at both ends — gentle with pings and ghosters, hands-off-the-pressure with hot leads, and fully out of negotiation mode for safety cases.
