# Book Profile — Getting Past No

> Pass 1 extraction for the marketplace selling copilot (Depop / Vinted / Poshmark / eBay Best-Offer + platform-agnostic core). Product material, not a summary.
> Source-supported content is cited with a chapter/quote locator. Items marked **[INFERRED]** are synthesized mappings to the marketplace domain (not in the book).

## 1. Metadata

- **title:** Getting Past No: Negotiating in Difficult Situations
- **author:** William Ury
- **category:** principled-negotiation / de-escalation (breakthrough negotiation school; companion to *Getting to Yes*)
- **domain_fit_note:** This is the de-escalation and reframing engine for hostile, lowballing, and stonewalling buyers. Ury's five-step "breakthrough strategy" — go to the balcony, step to their side, reframe, build a golden bridge, use power to educate — maps cleanly onto P2P resale chat where a seller faces rude DMs, insulting lowball Offers, "take it or leave it" haggling, and ghosting. Its weakness for this product is that the book is built around high-stakes, multi-session, often face-to-face disputes (labor strikes, hostage crises, treaty talks); the casual-seller use is single-thread, text-only, low-stakes, so the tactics must be compressed and the BATNA reframed as "relist / wait for another buyer."
- **applicability_score:** 4/5

---

## 2. Core thesis

Difficult negotiations stall not because the other side is irrational but because of five barriers: your own reaction, their emotion, their position, their dissatisfaction, and their power. You cannot bulldoze through these head-on — "the harder you make it for them to say no, the harder you make it for them to say yes" (the power paradox). The breakthrough move is indirect action: do the opposite of your instinct. Don't react (go to the balcony), don't argue (step to their side), don't reject (reframe their position as a shared problem), don't push (build them a golden bridge so saying yes is easy and face-saving), and don't escalate (use power to *educate* about the cost of no-deal, not to threaten). The aim throughout is to turn a face-to-face confrontation into side-by-side joint problem-solving — soft on the people, hard on the problem.

## 3. Plays

```
- id: getting-past-no-play-01
  principle: Go to the balcony (don't react)
  what_it_means: When a message provokes you (rude, insulting, lowball), suspend your instinctive reaction — striking back, caving, or breaking off — and mentally step back before replying.
  why_it_matters: "The first casualty of an attack is your objectivity." Reacting hooks you into the other side's game and perpetuates the action-reaction cycle; a few seconds' pause lets the biochemistry of anger dissipate so you can keep your eyes on the prize (a sale better than your walkaway).
  when_to_use: The instant a buyer message lands that makes you want to fire back — insults, a derisive lowball, "lol is this a joke," accusations the item is fake/overpriced.
  when_NOT_to_use: Not a reply tactic itself — it gates the others. Don't let "balcony" become indefinite silence that reads as ghosting; pause to compose, then respond.
  mechanic: |
    1. Name the game: is this a stone wall ("won't budge / firm"), an attack (insult/threat), or a trick (fake deadline, phantom partner)? Naming it breaks its spell.
    2. Buy time: pause and say nothing for a beat; never decide on the spot.
    3. Rewind the tape if needed: "Let me make sure I understand what you're asking."
    4. Don't make the accept/decline decision while angry — draft, reread, then send.
  marketplace_mapping: The copilot's first internal step on any flagged-hostile message: classify tactic, insert a cooling delay/draft step, and never auto-fire a defensive counter. In-app: a "take a breath — here's a calm draft" suggestion instead of letting the seller hit send on a heated reply.
  example_buyer_message: "$10. take it or leave it, this isn't worth what you're asking lol"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Thanks for the offer! Let me take a look at where I can land on price and get back to you in a sec."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 1 'Go to the Balcony' — 'the balcony is a metaphor for a mental attitude of detachment'; 'Don't get mad, don't get even, get what you want.'"
  product_stage_tags: [intent-detection, reply-gen, objection, safety, coaching]

- id: getting-past-no-play-02
  principle: Name the game (recognize the tactic)
  what_it_means: Privately label what the buyer is doing — stone wall, attack, or trick — so you respond to the tactic, not the bait.
  why_it_matters: "Many ploys depend on your not knowing what is being done to you." Recognizing a "take it or leave it" as a stone wall stops you believing it's truly final; recognizing a fake deadline as a trick stops you panicking.
  when_to_use: Any message with pressure cues: ultimatums, threats to leave a bad review, "my friend sells these for $X," "I need it tonight or never."
  when_NOT_to_use: Don't become paranoid — "put on your radar, not your armor." Some buyers are clumsy, not manipulative; treat the label as a possibility, not a verdict.
  marketplace_mapping: Feeds the intent classifier: tag each inbound as stone-wall / attack / trick (or sincere) and route to the matching reframe. Surfaces a quiet coaching note to the seller: "This reads like a 'take it or leave it' pressure move — it's usually not actually final."
  example_buyer_message: "Other sellers have this for way less. You have until tonight or I'm buying elsewhere."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Totally fair to shop around! If you find the same condition for less I get it. Mine's priced for [reason] — happy to hold a quick chat on it whenever works for you."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 1 'Name the Game' — 'Three Kinds of Tactics': stone walls, attacks, tricks; 'put on your radar, not your armor.'"
  product_stage_tags: [intent-detection, objection, safety]

- id: getting-past-no-play-03
  principle: Step to their side — listen and give them a hearing
  what_it_means: Before defending your price, let the buyer fully voice their concern and show you heard it. "Listening to someone may be the cheapest concession you can make."
  why_it_matters: "We all feel a deep need to be understood." A full hearing defuses hostility and makes the buyer far more willing to then hear your terms — the surprise of being heard instead of fought disarms them.
  when_to_use: Buyer is venting, frustrated, or has piled up several complaints (condition, price, shipping) in one message.
  when_NOT_to_use: When the buyer just made a clean offer you'll accept — don't manufacture a "tell me more" detour; close.
  marketplace_mapping: Reply-gen pattern that opens with acknowledgment before any counter. Discourages the copilot from leading with "No" or with price defense on emotionally charged messages.
  example_buyer_message: "This is overpriced. Secondhand stuff shouldn't cost this much and the photos are dark. I'm losing patience."
  recommended_agent_reply: "I hear you — you want to be sure it's worth it and that you can actually see what you're getting. That's totally reasonable. Let me add brighter photos, and I'll walk you through why it's priced where it is."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 2 'Step to Their Side' / 'Listen Actively' — 'Listening to someone may be the cheapest concession you can make.'"
  product_stage_tags: [objection, reply-gen, trust]

- id: getting-past-no-play-04
  principle: Paraphrase and acknowledge their point
  what_it_means: Reflect the buyer's concern back in your own words and acknowledge it as valid ("I can see how you see things") — without agreeing it's true.
  why_it_matters: Acknowledgment "begins to lose its emotional charge" from their complaint and creates psychological room for them to consider your side. It satisfies the need for recognition that fuels rigid positions.
  when_to_use: After a buyer objection, before you state your position; especially when you're about to say no to a discount.
  when_NOT_to_use: If it reads as parroting or insincere — "the other person will usually be able to tell whether or not your acknowledgment is sincere." Keep it short in chat.
  marketplace_mapping: Standard pre-amble in objection replies. Copilot template: paraphrase the buyer's ask in one line, then "Yes…and" into the seller's position (see play-06).
  example_buyer_message: "I'm not paying $45 for a used hoodie."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Fair — $45 for something pre-loved is a real consideration, I get that. Here's where I'm coming from on the price…"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 2 'Acknowledge Their Point' / 'Paraphrase and Ask for Corrections' — 'I can see how you see things'; the answering-machine customer example."
  product_stage_tags: [objection, reply-gen, pricing, trust]

- id: getting-past-no-play-05
  principle: Agree wherever you can / accumulate yeses
  what_it_means: Find any point you can genuinely say "yes" to — even small or humorous — to build agreement momentum without conceding price.
  why_it_matters: "Yes is a magic word." Each yes reduces tension and "creates an atmosphere in which they are more likely to say yes to a substantive proposal." It's hard to keep attacking someone who keeps agreeing with you.
  when_to_use: Adversarial or skeptical buyers; openings of tense threads where you want to flip the tone fast.
  when_NOT_to_use: Don't say yes to things that imply a discount you can't give; "agree without conceding."
  marketplace_mapping: Reply-gen tone control — copilot is biased to find a true point of agreement ("yes, shipping does add up"; "yes, there are cheaper ones out there") before the "and here's my position."
  example_buyer_message: "These are everywhere online for cheaper."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Yes — you can definitely find cheaper listings out there, you're right. And what you're getting here is [verified condition / fast shipping / exact size], which is where mine sits a little higher."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 2 'Agree Wherever You Can' / 'Accumulate Yeses' — 'Yes is a magic word, a powerful tool for disarming the other side.'"
  product_stage_tags: [objection, reply-gen, pricing]

- id: getting-past-no-play-06
  principle: Say "Yes…and," never "but"
  what_it_means: Express your differing view as an addition ("yes, and…") rather than a contradiction ("but…"), and use "I" statements over "you" statements.
  why_it_matters: "But" signals "I think you're wrong" and makes the buyer stop listening; "and" presents your position as coexisting with theirs. "I" statements describe impact on you and are hard to reject.
  when_to_use: Whenever you must disagree on price/condition/terms while keeping the buyer engaged.
  when_NOT_to_use: Don't disguise an accusation as an I-statement ("I feel that you're being unfair" is still a you-statement).
  marketplace_mapping: A hard formatting rule for the reply generator: strip "but," replace with "and"; convert "you're wrong about the price" → "the way I see it, the price reflects…". 
  example_buyer_message: "Your price is too high for the quality."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Yes, the price is at the higher end — and that's because it's [barely-worn / authenticated / comes with the box]. The way I see it, that's where the value is."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 2 'Don't Say But, Say Yes…And' and 'Make I Statements, Not You Statements.'"
  product_stage_tags: [objection, reply-gen, pricing]

- id: getting-past-no-play-07
  principle: Reframe — turn their position into a shared problem
  what_it_means: Don't reject a lowball or demand; recast it as a problem to solve together by asking a problem-solving question ("Help me understand…"; "How do we get you a fair price on this?").
  why_it_matters: "To change the game, change the frame." Reframing shifts the conversation from clashing positions to interests and options; the buyer often goes along because they're surprised you didn't reject them and eager to explain themselves.
  when_to_use: The core move on any lowball or rigid demand — the moment a number or ultimatum lands.
  when_NOT_to_use: When the gap is so absurd and the buyer so abusive that engagement just invites more abuse — then warn/relist (play-12).
  mechanic: |
    1. Accept, don't reject: "That's interesting."
    2. Probe behind the position: "Why that number? / What's your budget for this?"
    3. Recast as joint task: "Let's see how we get you something that works for both of us."
    4. Introduce options with "What if…?" (bundle, wait for sale, lower-priced alt).
  marketplace_mapping: The signature lowball-handler. On eBay/Posh Offer buttons and Depop/Vinted DMs, the copilot replies to a lowball not with a flat decline but with a reframe + a "what if" counter (bundle, small move, or hold-for-sale).
  example_buyer_message: "Offer: $20" (listed at $45)
  recommended_agent_reply: "Appreciate the offer! Help me understand — is $20 the budget you're working with, or were you hoping I'd meet you partway? If it's budget, I might have a cheaper piece that fits. On this one I could do $38."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 3 'Reframe' — 'To Change the Game, Change the Frame'; 'put a problem-solving frame around the other side's positional statements.'"
  product_stage_tags: [objection, reply-gen, pricing, intent-detection]

- id: getting-past-no-play-08
  principle: Ask open-ended problem-solving questions (Why / Why not / What if / What's fair)
  what_it_means: Replace assertions with open questions that can't be answered "no" — prefaced with how/why/what/who — to surface the buyer's real interest and budget.
  why_it_matters: "People are usually more convinced by reasons they discovered by themselves." Questions make the buyer reveal their constraints and reduce resistance better than you arguing. Yes/no questions ("can you do better?") set you up for a no.
  when_to_use: Early intent-detection; whenever you need to know the buyer's true budget or why they hesitate.
  when_NOT_to_use: Don't interrogate — one or two questions, not a barrage, in a casual chat.
  mechanic: |
    - Why: "What's making you hesitate?" / "Why that price?"
    - Why not: "What would be wrong with [bundle / small move]?"
    - What if: "What if I included shipping?" / "What if you grabbed two?"
    - What's fair: "What are you basing that on — have you seen the same condition for less?"
  marketplace_mapping: Powers the budget-discovery step before any concession; the copilot prefers an open question over a blind price drop, so the seller never concedes more than needed.
  example_buyer_message: "Can you do better on the price?"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Maybe! What were you hoping to spend? That helps me see what I can do."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 3 'Ask Problem-Solving Questions' and 'Make Your Questions Open-Ended' — 'ask a question that cannot be answered by no.'"
  product_stage_tags: [intent-detection, reply-gen, pricing, objection]

- id: getting-past-no-play-09
  principle: Ask "What makes that fair?" (appeal to a standard)
  what_it_means: When a demand seems unreasonable, don't reject it — ask what standard makes it fair, then let market/comp evidence do the persuading.
  why_it_matters: Standards (market price, comps, condition) let the buyer defer to something objective instead of "giving in to you." It depersonalizes the price and reduces ego clash.
  when_to_use: "Competitor is cheaper," "this should cost less," aggressive lowballs justified by a claim.
  when_NOT_to_use: If you actually are priced above market — the standard will cut against you; then adjust or justify with condition.
  marketplace_mapping: Copilot can cite sold-comps / "similar condition listings" as the fair standard and ask the buyer for theirs. Maps to eBay sold listings, Poshmark/Depop "similar items."
  example_buyer_message: "This is way overpriced, I've seen them for $25."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Could be — what condition were those in? The ones I'm seeing sold in this condition are landing around $40–45, which is how I set mine. If you've got a $25 one in the same shape, send it my way, I'd be curious!"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 3 'Ask What Makes That Fair?' — 'You must have good reasons for thinking that's a fair solution. I'd like to hear them.'"
  product_stage_tags: [objection, pricing, reply-gen, trust]

- id: getting-past-no-play-10
  principle: Expose tricks by playing dumb like a fox
  what_it_means: Don't accuse a buyer of a trick (fake deadline, phantom "my partner won't let me," last-minute add-on); play along, ask clarifying questions, and make a reasonable-request test.
  why_it_matters: Direct accusation may be wrong and will damage the relationship; probing questions expose the trick while letting the buyer save face by pretending it was a misunderstanding. Tricksters have "a stake in appearing reasonable."
  when_to_use: "I'll buy but only if you also throw in X" after a deal; "my friend can get it cheaper"; sudden urgent deadline.
  when_NOT_to_use: Clear scams (off-platform payment, "pay this link") — that's a safety stop, not a negotiation (see ignore-list / guardrails).
  marketplace_mapping: Handles the last-minute add-on and phantom-third-party haggle. On a post-agreement extra demand, copilot asks "Are we reopening the deal, or sticking with what we agreed?" If buyer cites a cheaper friend, "reasonable request": "Send me their listing — if it's the same, I'll match!"
  example_buyer_message: "Ok deal at $40 — oh and can you include the matching bag for free?"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Happy you're in! Quick check — are we reopening the price, or keeping our $40 deal? The bag's a separate item, but I could do the pair for $X if you want both."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 3 'Expose Tricks' — 'play dumb like a fox'; 'reasonable request test'; the last-minute-demand handling."
  product_stage_tags: [objection, safety, reply-gen, pricing]

- id: getting-past-no-play-11
  principle: Build a golden bridge — make yes easy and face-saving
  what_it_means: Don't push the buyer toward your terms; draw them across by involving their ideas, meeting an unmet interest, and letting them retreat from their lowball without looking like they lost.
  why_it_matters: Resistance usually comes from "not their idea," an unmet interest, fear of losing face, or "too much too fast." Pushing widens the chasm; a golden bridge reframes their retreat as an advance. Face is "self-worth… the wish to act consistently with past statements."
  when_to_use: Buyer is close but stuck after holding a hard line publicly (e.g., commented "$20 max" under the listing) and now needs a way up without backing down.
  when_NOT_to_use: When no real interest of theirs exists to meet and they simply want a price you can't give — don't invent a bridge that loses you money.
  mechanic: |
    1. Involve them: ask for / build on their idea ("what works for you?").
    2. Meet an unmet interest: bundle, free shipping, hold until payday, gift-wrap.
    3. Help them save face: "Prices just dropped" / "since you're grabbing two" / "new-buyer deal" — give a reason the move isn't a climb-down.
    4. Go slow to go fast: small yeses, don't ask final commitment till the end.
  marketplace_mapping: The concession-with-cover pattern. Instead of "fine, $20," the copilot frames a move as a bundle deal, a just-launched sale, or a multi-item discount so the buyer "wins." Maps to Posh/Depop bundles, eBay "send offer to likers," Vinted bundle discount.
  example_buyer_message: "I told you $25 is my max."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Tell you what — I'm running a little bundle deal this week, so if you add the [matching item] I can do the pair for $40, which gets this one down near your number. Want me to set that up?"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 4 'Build Them a Golden Bridge' — 'reframe a retreat from their position as an advance toward a better solution'; 'Help Them Save Face'; 'Help Write Their Victory Speech.'"
  product_stage_tags: [pricing, objection, reply-gen, trust]

- id: getting-past-no-play-12
  principle: Use power to educate, not threaten — warn with your BATNA (relist)
  what_it_means: If the buyer won't move, don't threaten ("I'll just sell to someone else, bye"); state the consequence of no-deal objectively as a warning, keeping your offer on the table.
  why_it_matters: "A threat appears subjective and confrontational, while a warning appears objective and respectful." Threats trigger ego and fighting back; a warning ("this size sells fast, I'll likely relist") lets reality educate them while the golden bridge stays open. Power paradox: cornering them makes yes harder.
  when_to_use: Final stage of a haggle where the buyer is stalling or testing whether you'll cave; your real BATNA is to relist / wait for another buyer.
  when_NOT_to_use: As a first move (reads aggressive), or when relisting genuinely isn't a good alternative (item slow-moving) — don't bluff a BATNA you don't have.
  mechanic: |
    1. Reality-test question: "What do you think happens if we can't agree?"
    2. Warn, don't threaten: "If we don't land on a price, I'll probably just keep it listed — these tend to go at asking within a week."
    3. Keep the bridge in view: restate your best offer so the easy way out stays visible.
    4. Let them choose: "$38 stands whenever you're ready — no pressure either way."
  marketplace_mapping: Converts the relist option into gentle leverage. The copilot frames "I'll relist / others are interested" as a neutral fact, never an insult, and always re-anchors the standing offer. Maps to eBay relist, "X people have this in their cart/likes," Depop/Posh relist-and-share.
  example_buyer_message: "I'm not going a cent over $25. Final."
  recommended_agent_reply: "All good! I'll keep it at $38 for now — a couple people have it liked, so it may sell soon, but if you change your mind it's yours at $38. No worries either way."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 5 'Use Power to Educate' — 'Warn, Don't Threaten'; 'use power to bring them to their senses, not to their knees'; reality-testing questions."
  product_stage_tags: [pricing, objection, reply-gen, safety]

- id: getting-past-no-play-13
  principle: Keep sharpening the choice — leave the offer on the table
  what_it_means: When a buyer refuses, resist withdrawing your best offer in anger; leave it visible so the contrast between "no deal" and "your easy way in" stays sharp.
  why_it_matters: "Nothing will do more to reduce resistance than the possibility of an attractive way out." Your leverage comes from the contrast between consequences and the golden bridge, not from punishing them.
  when_to_use: After a buyer says no or goes quiet post-haggle; re-engagement of a stalled thread.
  when_NOT_to_use: If the offer was a one-time courtesy that's now expired and re-offering trains the buyer to wait you out — then let it lapse cleanly.
  marketplace_mapping: Drives a follow-up/re-engagement template: a low-pressure nudge that restates the standing offer rather than escalating. Maps to eBay "Send offer," Posh "offer to likers," a polite Depop/Vinted check-in.
  example_buyer_message: "[no reply for 2 days after declining]"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Hey! Just circling back — my offer of $38 (with free shipping) still stands if you're interested. Totally fine if not, happy to help either way."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 5 'Keep Sharpening Their Choice' / 'Let Them Choose' — 'leave your most generous offer on the table in full view'; 'respect their freedom to choose.'"
  product_stage_tags: [reply-gen, pricing, learning-loop]

- id: getting-past-no-play-14
  principle: Aim for mutual satisfaction, not victory — be generous at the end
  what_it_means: Even when you have the upper hand, don't squeeze the last dollar or gloat; let the buyer feel they did well so they complete the purchase, leave a good review, and come back.
  why_it_matters: "An imposed outcome is an unstable one." A buyer who feels crushed may renege, leave bad feedback, or never return; "we like them to win the last round." Satisfaction pays off in implementation and repeat business.
  when_to_use: Closing any deal, especially after a tense haggle; deciding whether to nickel-and-dime a final small request.
  when_NOT_to_use: Don't confuse generosity with being exploited — give the last small crumb, not your whole margin.
  marketplace_mapping: Closing-message template and a margin guardrail: copilot biases toward conceding a tiny final ask (free shipping, a sticker, fast dispatch) to lock the sale and seed a 5-star review / repeat follow. Feeds the learning-loop on which closers correlate with reviews/repeat buyers.
  example_buyer_message: "Ok I'll take it at $38. Can you ship today?"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Done — $38 it is, and yes I'll get it out today. Thanks so much, I think you got a great deal on this one. I'll send tracking once it's on its way!"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch. 5 'Aim for Mutual Satisfaction, Not Victory' — 'we save some flexibility for the end because we like them to win the last round'; 'don't crow.'"
  product_stage_tags: [reply-gen, trust, pricing, learning-loop, metrics]

- id: getting-past-no-play-15
  principle: Prepare the prize and the BATNA before you reply
  what_it_means: Before negotiating, know your interests (ranked), your walkaway (relist value / floor price), and three target prices: aspiration, content-with, and live-with-but-barely.
  why_it_matters: "Most negotiations are won or lost even before the talking begins." A known BATNA is the source of negotiating power and a "trip wire" against accepting a deal worse than relisting. Low aspirations are self-fulfilling — "aim high."
  when_to_use: At listing creation and at the first sign of haggling; sets the floor the copilot will defend.
  when_NOT_to_use: Don't over-prepare a casual $15 item into a strategy session — scale effort to stakes.
  mechanic: |
    1. Interests, ranked: cash now vs. best price vs. clearing space vs. relationship/repeat buyer.
    2. BATNA: realistic relist outcome (how fast, at what price) = your floor.
    3. Three numbers: aspiration (list price), content-with (target accept), live-with (floor = just above BATNA).
    4. The floor is a trip wire: below it, warn + relist rather than accept.
  marketplace_mapping: The copilot's pricing brain. At listing time it computes/asks for floor + targets, then never concedes below floor; uses comps as the standard. Powers auto-decline thresholds on eBay/Posh Offers and the "minimum acceptable" used in counters.
  example_buyer_message: "[listing just posted; first lowball arrives at $20 on a $45 item, floor $35]"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Thanks for the offer! I can't go to $20 on this, but I could do $38. My lowest would be $35 — want it at $35?"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Prologue 'Prepare, Prepare, Prepare' — interests/options/standards/alternatives (BATNA)/proposals; 'BATNA is the key to negotiating power'; three proposals: aspire / content / live-with."
  product_stage_tags: [pricing, listing, coaching, metrics]
```

| Reasonable-request test | Expose a trick while saving face | "Send me their listing and I'll match"; "Are we reopening the deal or keeping it?" | Overuse makes you seem suspicious of everyone | "Send the cheaper one — if it's the same I'll match!" | "You're lying, no one sells it cheaper." | Ch. 3 'Expose Tricks' |

## 5. Objection material

| objection_key | handling (book's approach) | sample_reply |
|---|---|---|
| `rude-aggressive` | Go to the balcony (don't react); name the game; step to their side with acknowledgment; respond to the problem, not the insult. (Ch. 1–2) | "Thanks for the offer! Let me see where I can land — I want to find something that works for you." |
| `lowball` | Don't reject — reframe as a shared problem; probe budget with an open question; counter with a "what if" (bundle / small move / cheaper alt). (Ch. 3) | "Appreciate it! Is $20 your budget, or were you hoping I'd meet you partway? On this one I could do $38 — or I've got a cheaper piece if that helps." |
| `price-too-high` | Acknowledge ("yes, it's at the higher end"), then "yes…and" with the value; appeal to a fair standard (comps/condition). (Ch. 2–3) | "Yes, it's priced higher than some — and that's because it's barely worn and authenticated. Similar-condition ones are selling around $40–45." |
| `competitor-cheaper` | Ask "what makes that fair?" / "what condition?"; reasonable-request test ("send it, I'll match if it's the same"). (Ch. 3) | "Could be! What condition were they in? If you find the same shape for less, send it over and I'll see what I can do." |
| `wants-bundle-deal` | Use as a golden bridge / low-cost-high-benefit trade; let the bundle be the face-saving cover for a price move. (Ch. 4) | "Love it — if you add the [matching item], I can do the pair for $40, which brings this one closer to your number." |
| `stalling-ghosting` | Keep the offer on the table; sharpen the choice with a low-pressure warning (relist) + restate best offer; let them choose. (Ch. 5) | "Just circling back — $38 with free shipping still stands. A couple people have it liked so it may sell soon, but no pressure!" |
| `condition-doubt` | Step to their side; acknowledge the concern; offer evidence (more photos) as the fair standard; apologize for any genuine gap. (Ch. 2) | "Totally fair to want a closer look — I'll add brighter photos of [area] right now so you can see exactly what you're getting." |
| `shipping-cost` | Acknowledge ("yes, shipping adds up"); reframe with a "what if" (free shipping as the bridge / bundle to absorb it). (Ch. 2–4) | "Yes, shipping does add up — what if I cover it? That effectively knocks a few dollars off for you." |
| `is-it-available` | Low-friction yes + gentle forward motion (don't over-negotiate a ready buyer). (general — Ch. 4 'go slow to go fast' inverse: don't stall a yes) | "Yes, it's available! Want me to send it over / hold it for you?" |
| `off-platform-request` | NOT a negotiation — safety stop; the book's BATNA/warning logic doesn't apply to scams. (see Guardrails) | "I keep all sales on-platform for both our protection — happy to finish the deal right here!" |
| `trust-authenticity` | Acknowledge; provide an objective standard (receipts, authentication, detailed photos); reaffirm with confidence, not defensiveness. (Ch. 2–3) | "Great question — it's authenticated and I've got the receipt; I'll add photos of the tags and code so you can verify." |

## 6. Buyer psychology insights

- The buyer's rigid position usually masks an underlying *interest* (budget ceiling, fear of being ripped off, desire to feel they "won"). Probe the interest, not the position. (Prologue; Ch. 3)
- A buyer who feels heard becomes "less reactive, more rational, and more responsive" — being listened to is itself a concession they value. (Ch. 2)
- Buyers cling to positions partly to avoid *losing face* in front of an audience (a public comment, a friend they bragged to). Give them a graceful exit and they'll move. (Ch. 4)
- "People are usually more convinced by reasons they discovered by themselves" — buyers persuaded by their own answer to a question resist far less than buyers you lecture. (Ch. 3)
- Hostile/abusive buyers are often *trying to make you react* to throw you off balance and extract a concession; their power comes from your reaction. (Ch. 1)
- A buyer overestimates their own BATNA ("I'll just buy it cheaper elsewhere") and underestimates yours; gentle reality-testing recalibrates both. (Ch. 5)
- "Too much too fast" causes a no — a big single decision feels riskier than a series of small yeses; sequence the agreement. (Ch. 4)
- Even seemingly irrational buyers are rational from their own frame; behind a "crazy" demand is usually an unmet need (recognition, security, control). (Ch. 4)

## 7. Seller psychology insights (biases the copilot should counter)

- **Reaction reflex:** the seller's instinct to strike back at a rude/lowball message is the single biggest mistake; the copilot should interpose a cooling/draft step. (Ch. 1)
- **Caving to end discomfort:** sellers give in just to stop an unpleasant exchange, "rewarding bad behavior" and inviting future lowballs. (Ch. 1)
- **Either/or mindset:** sellers frame it as "I win or they win" and miss joint-gain options (bundle, shipping, hold-for-payday). Push a both/and frame. (Ch. 2)
- **Leading with "but":** sellers contradict and make buyers stop listening; enforce "yes…and." (Ch. 2)
- **Pushing too hard:** sellers cajole/pressure a hesitant buyer, widening the gap; the copilot should pull (golden bridge) not push. (Ch. 4)
- **Threatening instead of warning:** sellers blurt "I'll just sell to someone else" as an insult; reframe as a neutral warning that keeps the door open. (Ch. 5)
- **Low aspirations / under-pricing:** sellers set timid prices and accept the first lowball; "aim high," know your floor, defend it. (Prologue)
- **Crowing / squeezing the last dollar:** sellers nickel-and-dime or gloat at close, risking reneging and bad reviews; be generous at the end. (Ch. 5)
- **No floor / no BATNA:** sellers negotiate without a walkaway and drift below what relisting would net; set the trip-wire floor first. (Prologue)

## 8. Ignore-list (considered and dropped for this product)

- **Hostage/crisis & terrorist negotiation** examples (Iranian hostages, bank robbers, Berlin women's march) — illustrative only; mechanics are abstracted, the scenarios are out of scope.
- **Labor/union strikes, treaty & geopolitical talks** (SALT II, Cuban missile crisis, Chrysler bailout) — multi-constituency, high-stakes; not casual-resale.
- **Enterprise B2B mergers & acquisitions** (CBS–Gannett, Campbell Soup) — used for the golden-bridge principle but the deal mechanics don't transfer.
- **In-person body-language tactics** (mirroring posture, sitting side-by-side, "tune in to their wavelength" via physical cues) — irrelevant in text-only chat; only the verbal mirroring of language/sensory-words partially transfers.
- **Bringing a negotiating partner / caucus / taking a physical time-out / rehearsing with a colleague** — preparation mechanics that don't fit a solo casual seller in async chat (the *principle* of pre-thinking is kept in play-15).
- **Formal dispute-resolution clauses, escrow, arbitration, golden-parachute contracts** — over-engineered for low-value resale (platform buyer-protection already covers this).
- **Third-party coalitions / mediators / "tap the third force"** — no natural third party in a 1:1 marketplace DM (closest analog = platform support for disputes, noted under safety, not as a negotiation lever).

## 9. Guardrail flags

- **Face-saving covers must be true, not fabricated.** "Prices just dropped" or "new-buyer deal" (play-11) is fine only if genuine; inventing a fake sale or fake "other interested buyers" is fake-urgency / deception and erodes trust. Flag any fabricated scarcity.
- **Warnings must not become threats.** The relist-BATNA (play-12) is ethical only when stated as a neutral fact and the BATNA is real; bluffing "others are buying it" when no one is is manipulative. (Ch. 5 distinction)
- **Reasonable-request test ≠ calling the buyer a liar.** Keep it face-saving; don't weaponize it to shame.
- **Don't engineer false "yeses."** Accumulating agreement (play-05) is for genuine common ground, not tricking the buyer into a sale path they didn't intend.
- **Safety overrides negotiation.** Off-platform payment, "pay this link," code-sharing, or pressure to ship before payment are scam signals — the copilot must hard-stop and refuse to "negotiate," not apply balcony/bridge logic.
- **Don't exploit a vulnerable or confused buyer.** The book's empathy is sincere; the copilot should not use acknowledgment/face-saving to push someone into overpaying beyond fair market.
