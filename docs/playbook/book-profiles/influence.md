# Book Profile — Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion

## 1. Metadata
- **title:** Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion
- **author:** Robert B. Cialdini
- **category:** persuasion
- **slug:** influence
- **domain_fit_note:** Cialdini's six principles map almost 1:1 onto the levers a casual reseller already pulls — social proof (sold counts, likes, reviews), scarcity (one-of-a-kind item, others have it saved), authority (proof of authenticity), liking (friendly rapport), reciprocity (free shipping / a little extra), and commitment (small yeses → purchase). This makes it the single best source for *listing copy* and *trust framing*. It is also the highest guardrail-risk book in the set: every principle has an honest version and a manipulative version, and the manipulative versions are exactly the dark patterns marketplaces (and platform policies) penalize.
- **applicability_score:** 5

## 2. Core thesis
People face an information-saturated world and so lean on mental shortcuts ("click, whirr") that let one trigger feature stand in for a full judgment. Six such shortcuts — reciprocation, commitment & consistency, social proof, liking, authority, and scarcity — reliably produce compliance because each is usually a sensible rule of thumb. Compliance professionals exploit these by manufacturing the trigger feature without the underlying reality (fake scarcity, salted tip jars, counterfeit authority). The defense, and the ethical line, is whether the trigger is *genuine*: the principles work as well honestly as dishonestly, so a seller never needs to fake them.

```
- id: influence-play-01
  principle: Social proof — quantified demand
  what_it_means: People judge what is correct/desirable by what others are doing; "many others chose this" is treated as proof of quality.
  why_it_matters: Removes the buyer's uncertainty without you having to argue the item is good — "95 percent of people are imitators." Lowers perceived risk on a stranger's listing.
  when_to_use: Listing has real traction: genuine sold counts, multiple likes/saves, repeat buyers, positive reviews.
  when_NOT_to_use: No real demand yet — do NOT invent it. Fabricated counts are the canonical Cialdini abuse (salted tip jars, disco lines, telethon ringers).
  marketplace_mapping: Surface true numbers in the listing/first reply — Depop/Posh likes, "12 people have this saved," "sold 3 of these already," star rating + review count, "from a 200+ sale shop."
  example_buyer_message: "Is this actually any good? bit nervous buying secondhand."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Totally fair! For what it's worth, 14 people have this saved and I've sold two of the same style this month — both buyers left 5 stars. Happy to send extra photos too."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.4 Social Proof — 'we determine what is correct... by find[ing] out what other people think is correct'; bartenders salting tip jars, '95 percent... are imitators.'"
  product_stage_tags: [listing, trust, reply-gen, objection]

- id: influence-play-02
  principle: Social proof under uncertainty + similarity
  what_it_means: Social proof is strongest when (a) the buyer is uncertain and (b) the proof comes from people *like them*.
  why_it_matters: A nervous first-time buyer is exactly the uncertain audience the principle targets; "people like you bought this" beats a generic count.
  when_to_use: Buyer signals doubt/inexperience; you can honestly cite a similar buyer ("another size-S buyer," "someone styling it for a wedding too").
  when_NOT_to_use: You have no comparable real buyer to reference — don't fabricate a persona.
  marketplace_mapping: In reply-gen, tailor the proof: "most people buying this are vintage collectors like you" only if your sales actually skew that way.
  example_buyer_message: "I've never bought trainers secondhand before, do they come up small?"
  recommended_agent_reply: "You're in good company — a lot of first-time secondhand buyers grab these. The last two buyers were also a UK 8 and both said they fit true to size."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.4 Social Proof — proof works best amid uncertainty and from similar others (O'Connor film study; 'a variety of other children')."
  product_stage_tags: [trust, reply-gen, objection, intent-detection]

- id: influence-play-03
  principle: Scarcity — limited supply (genuine)
  what_it_means: Opportunities feel more valuable as they become less available; "rare = better."
  why_it_matters: Drives the buyer to act now instead of indefinitely "thinking about it." One-of-a-kind resale items are *genuinely* scarce, so this is honest here.
  when_to_use: The item truly is one-of-one, last size, deadstock, or you genuinely have multiple interested buyers.
  when_NOT_to_use: Mass-available item, or you're inventing competing buyers. Faking "only one left" / restocking the "sold out" item is the appliance-store scam.
  marketplace_mapping: State the true fact: "this is the only one I have and I won't be restocking," "it's the last M." Note real saves: "a few people have it saved." Never a fake countdown timer.
  example_buyer_message: "Might come back for this next payday."
  recommended_agent_reply: "No pressure at all — just so you know it's the only one I have and it's not being restocked, and a couple of people have it saved. Happy to answer anything in the meantime."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.7 Scarcity — 'opportunities seem more valuable to us when their availability is limited'; the 'limited-number' tactic ('sometimes... true, sometimes... wholly false')."
  product_stage_tags: [listing, pricing, objection, reply-gen]

- id: influence-play-04
  principle: Scarcity — loss framing (potential loss > equal gain)
  what_it_means: People are more motivated to avoid losing something than to gain the same thing; frame around what they'd miss.
  why_it_matters: "You could own this" is weaker than "you'd miss out on this." Directly lifts conversion on a fence-sitting buyer.
  when_to_use: Genuinely limited item and a hesitating buyer; frame the real, true loss.
  when_NOT_to_use: When the "loss" is invented (it'll restock, you have ten). Manufactured loss = manipulation.
  marketplace_mapping: Reframe a wishlist reply: instead of "it's available," say "didn't want you to miss it since it's the last one" — only when true.
  example_buyer_message: "Still deciding between this and another seller's."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Makes sense to compare! Only flag: mine's the last in this colourway, so I'd hate for you to miss it if you do land on this one. Either way no worries."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.7 Scarcity — 'people seem to be more motivated by the thought of losing something than... gaining something of equal value' (insulation / breast-self-exam studies)."
  product_stage_tags: [pricing, objection, reply-gen]

- id: influence-play-05
  principle: Reciprocity — the small genuine gift
  what_it_means: We feel obliged to repay what we receive; an unsolicited favor creates a sense of debt (and can even override dislike of the giver).
  why_it_matters: A small, real extra (free shipping, a sample, a discount code, a thrown-in accessory) generates goodwill and often a return purchase or a smoother negotiation — Regan's Coke study: favor recipients bought twice as many tickets.
  when_to_use: To warm up a cold lead, reward a repeat buyer, or break a stalemate — when you can genuinely afford the extra.
  when_NOT_to_use: As a manufactured obligation designed to trap ("I gave you free shipping so you OWE me a 5-star review"). Pressuring on the debt crosses into the Krishna-flower zone.
  mechanic: |
    1. Give first, unprompted, something small and real (free shipping, a freebie, a bundle discount).
    2. Name it plainly as a gift, no strings ("on me").
    3. Only AFTER, make your ask (accept offer, leave honest review, check out my other items) — and keep it optional.
    4. Never invoke the debt out loud ("you owe me"). The obligation works best unspoken.
  marketplace_mapping: eBay: "I'll cover shipping on this one." Depop/Vinted: toss in a matching scrunchie / sticker. Posh: a bundle discount applied before they ask.
  example_buyer_message: "Would you do a tiny bit less?"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Tell you what — I'll keep the price but cover shipping for you, that's on me. Works out about the same and saves you the hassle. Deal?"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.2 Reciprocation — Regan Coke study (favor recipients 'bought twice as many tickets'); free-sample / Amway BUG; Krishna flower as the abuse case."
  product_stage_tags: [pricing, objection, trust, reply-gen]

- id: influence-play-06
  principle: Reciprocity — rejection-then-retreat (concession sequence)
  what_it_means: After you refuse a buyer's first ask, your move from a larger position to a smaller one reads as a concession they feel obliged to reciprocate.
  why_it_matters: Tripled compliance in Cialdini's zoo-trip study (17%→50%); combines reciprocity AND contrast so your real price looks like a gift.
  when_to_use: Buyer lowballs; you want to land near your target without looking inflexible.
  when_NOT_to_use: If your opening counter is so high it looks unreasonable/bad-faith — the Bar-Ilan finding: an extreme first position backfires because the retreat isn't seen as a real concession.
  mechanic: |
    1. Hold a firm, reasonable anchor near (not absurdly above) your target.
    2. Decline the lowball without hostility.
    3. Retreat one visible step toward them and label it as a concession ("I can come down to X for you").
    4. Stop there; the reciprocity + contrast pressure pulls them up to meet you.
  marketplace_mapping: eBay Best-Offer / Depop offers: counter, then on their next nudge make ONE labeled move — "meet me halfway and it's yours."
  example_buyer_message: "$25?" (listed $60)
  recommended_agent_reply: "$25 is a bit low for me on this one — but because you came back, I'll do $45 instead of $60. That's a real drop for you. Shall I send the offer?"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.2 Reciprocation — 'rejection-then-retreat technique'; zoo-trip study 17%→50%; Bar-Ilan backfire when first request 'unreasonable.'"
  product_stage_tags: [pricing, objection, reply-gen]

- id: influence-play-07
  principle: Commitment & consistency — start with a small yes
  what_it_means: A small initial commitment shifts self-image and makes a later, larger consistent action more likely (foot-in-the-door).
  why_it_matters: A buyer who has liked, asked a question, or accepted a tiny agreement is psychologically primed to follow through to purchase. 76% vs 17% in the lawn-sign study.
  when_to_use: Early in a chat — invite a low-stakes yes (a question answered, a size confirmed, "want me to hold it for an hour?").
  when_NOT_to_use: Engineering a trivial commitment purely to trap someone into an unwanted purchase, or escalating pressure once they hesitate (dark-pattern territory).
  mechanic: |
    1. Secure an easy, genuine yes ("Is this for you or a gift?" / "Want measurements?").
    2. Acknowledge and build on it.
    3. Offer the next small step consistent with it ("Shall I hold it while you decide?").
    4. Let purchase be the natural consistent next move — never demand it.
  marketplace_mapping: Depop/Vinted DMs: answer-a-question → "want me to reserve it?" → checkout. The like itself is the first micro-commitment.
  example_buyer_message: "Do you have measurements?"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Yes! Pit-to-pit 52cm, length 70cm. Sounds like it'd fit you — want me to hold it for you for a bit while you check?"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.3 Commitment & Consistency — Freedman & Fraser foot-in-the-door (small sign → 76% accept big sign)."
  product_stage_tags: [intent-detection, reply-gen, objection]

- id: influence-play-08
  principle: Commitment & consistency — get it in writing / make it active
  what_it_means: Commitments that are active, public, and written-down bind harder than passive or private ones.
  why_it_matters: A buyer who types "yes I'll take it at $40" is far more likely to actually pay than one who merely thought it. Reduces ghosting after a verbal deal.
  when_to_use: Closing a negotiated deal in chat before sending the invoice/offer.
  when_NOT_to_use: To lock someone into a deal they're clearly unsure of, or to shame them later for "going back on their word."
  marketplace_mapping: Get the explicit confirmation in the thread ("confirming $40 + shipping, I'll send the offer now"), then send the eBay offer / Depop payment link immediately while the commitment is fresh.
  example_buyer_message: "ok yeah let's do 40"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Perfect — confirming $40 plus the $5 shipping, total $45. Sending you the offer now so you can check out. Thanks!"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.3 Commitment & Consistency — written/active/public commitments 'grow their own legs'; the more we commit the more we self-justify."
  product_stage_tags: [pricing, reply-gen, safety, learning-loop]

- id: influence-play-09
  principle: Authority — genuine proof / credibility
  what_it_means: We defer to legitimate expertise and credible signals; authority is a trusted shortcut for "this is right."
  why_it_matters: For a stranger selling online, demonstrated knowledge and real proof (receipts, serials, condition specifics) directly answer the trust/authenticity objection.
  when_to_use: Buyer questions authenticity, condition, or your competence; you genuinely have the proof or knowledge.
  when_NOT_to_use: Claiming expertise/credentials you don't have, or implying authentication you can't back. Counterfeit authority (the actor "playing a doctor," con-man titles) is the abuse.
  marketplace_mapping: Listing/trust copy: show the receipt photo, batch/serial codes, date code, stitching close-ups; cite specifics ("AF1 '07, the white tongue tag confirms it"); link your verified/authenticated badge if real.
  example_buyer_message: "How do I know this bag is real?"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Great question — I've added photos of the date code (inside the side pocket) and the original receipt. The hardware is engraved, not stamped, which is one of the real-vs-fake tells. Happy to photograph any specific detail you want."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.6 Authority — genuine vs symbolic authority; 'connotation, not content' (Robert Young as 'doctor'); titles/clothes/trappings counterfeited 'with the flimsiest of materials.'"
  product_stage_tags: [listing, trust, objection, reply-gen]

- id: influence-play-10
  principle: Liking — similarity & common ground
  what_it_means: We comply more with people we like, and we like people who are similar to us in background, taste, or interests.
  why_it_matters: A bit of genuine common ground ("I collect these too") warms the buyer and smooths negotiation. Cheap to build, powerful in effect.
  when_to_use: Throughout chat; mirror the buyer's tone and surface real shared interests.
  when_NOT_to_use: Manufacturing fake similarity ("oh I love that band too!" when you don't) — Cialdini explicitly warns about salespeople who "just seem to be just like you."
  marketplace_mapping: Match the buyer's vibe in DMs (emoji/casual vs precise), reference real shared taste ("I styled this exact jacket with Docs"), be a human not a script.
  example_buyer_message: "omg I've been hunting for this colourway forever 😭"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Honestly same, this colourway is so hard to find — I almost kept it 😅 You'll love it. Want me to grab a couple more pics in daylight?"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.5 Liking — 'We like people who are similar to us'; mirror-and-match warning ('just like you')."
  product_stage_tags: [reply-gen, trust, objection, intent-detection]

- id: influence-play-11
  principle: Liking — sincere compliments
  what_it_means: We like (and comply with) people who praise us; praise works even when we suspect a motive.
  why_it_matters: A genuine compliment on the buyer's taste/eye builds rapport and reciprocal warmth (Joe Girard's "I like you" cards).
  when_to_use: When the buyer shows good taste, a thoughtful question, or a fair offer — acknowledge it honestly.
  when_NOT_to_use: Empty/obvious flattery as a manipulation lever; insincere praise reads as a sales tactic and erodes trust.
  marketplace_mapping: "Great eye, this is one of my favourite pieces" / "Honestly a fair offer, I appreciate that" in reply-gen.
  example_buyer_message: "I'll offer $50, hope that's fair."
  recommended_agent_reply: "That's a really fair offer and I appreciate you not lowballing — you've also got great taste, this one always gets saved fast. Accepting now."
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.5 Liking — Compliments; 'we are phenomenal suckers for flattery'; Joe Girard 'I like you' cards."
  product_stage_tags: [reply-gen, objection, pricing]

- id: influence-play-12
  principle: Liking — familiarity & cooperation (toward a shared goal)
  what_it_means: Repeated positive contact and working *together* toward a common goal build liking; contact under conflict does the opposite.
  why_it_matters: Framing the negotiation as "us solving this together" rather than buyer-vs-seller defuses adversarial haggling and repeat-buyer relationships compound.
  when_to_use: Tense price talk, bundle requests, or nurturing repeat buyers.
  when_NOT_to_use: When "cooperation" is a veneer over pressure; forced contact under friction reduces liking, so don't badger.
  marketplace_mapping: "Let's find a number that works for both of us"; reward returning buyers with a loyalty discount; keep the tone collaborative in bundle haggling.
  example_buyer_message: "Can you do a deal if I take three items?"
  recommended_agent_reply: "Love that — let's build a bundle that works for both of us. Pick your three and I'll knock 15% off the total and combine shipping. Sound good?"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.5 Liking — Contact & Cooperation; familiarity raises liking, but contact under conflict 'leads to less liking' (Aronson jigsaw classroom)."
  product_stage_tags: [pricing, objection, trust, reply-gen, learning-loop]

- id: influence-play-13
  principle: Perceptual contrast (anchoring)
  what_it_means: An item seen right after a more expensive one looks cheaper than if seen alone; sequence shapes perceived price.
  why_it_matters: Lets you make your price or bundle feel like a deal by ordering what the buyer sees — the "expensive item first" rule.
  when_to_use: Presenting bundles or upsells; framing your firm price against comparable higher-priced listings.
  when_NOT_to_use: Using fake "compare-at" / phantom-high anchors (the realtor's "setup" dump houses, or a struck-through price that was never real) = deceptive anchoring.
  marketplace_mapping: Show the pricier comparable first ("similar ones go for $90, mine's $60"), or quote the bundle total against the sum of singles. Real comps only.
  example_buyer_message: "$60 feels like a lot."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Totally get it — for context the same jacket is listed at $85–95 by other sellers right now, so $60 in this condition is a genuine saving. Want me to send those links?"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.1 Weapons of Influence — contrast principle; clothiers 'sell the costly item first'; Phil's real-estate 'setup' houses (the abuse case)."
  product_stage_tags: [listing, pricing, objection, reply-gen]

- id: influence-play-14
  principle: Scarcity via reactance — protect the buyer's freedom
  what_it_means: When freedom/choice is threatened, people want the thing MORE (psychological reactance); heavy pressure to buy can backfire into refusal.
  why_it_matters: The counter-intuitive guardrail play: high-pressure "buy NOW or lose it forever" can trigger reactance and a walk-away. Giving the buyer their freedom ("no pressure") often closes better.
  when_to_use: With a hesitant or independent-minded buyer; whenever you feel tempted to push hard.
  when_NOT_to_use: Never replace this with manufactured can't-come-back pressure (the vacuum-cleaner "company policy" lie) — it's both abusive and self-defeating.
  marketplace_mapping: Default copilot tone: state the true scarcity once, then explicitly hand back control — "no rush, it'll be here." Avoid repeated nudges/auto-countdowns.
  example_buyer_message: "Stop messaging me, I'm still thinking."
  recommended_agent_reply: "Of course — no pressure at all, take your time. It's here whenever you're ready and I'm happy to answer anything. 🙂"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.7 Scarcity — Psychological Reactance (Brehm); Romeo-and-Juliet effect; can't-come-back claim exposed as 'nonsense.'"
  product_stage_tags: [safety, objection, reply-gen, coaching]

- id: influence-play-15
  principle: Authority — trappings/credibility of the shop itself
  what_it_means: Symbols of legitimacy (clean presentation, complete info, track record) trigger deference and trust even before the content is examined.
  why_it_matters: A polished, complete, well-reviewed shop reads as "legitimate authority," lowering buyer risk perception across every listing.
  when_to_use: Always, at the shop/listing level — it's ambient trust.
  when_NOT_to_use: Fabricating the trappings (fake badges, borrowed stock photos, invented "verified" claims) is counterfeit authority — flag and forbid.
  marketplace_mapping: Real, sharp photos; full measurements/flaws disclosed; consistent branding; genuine review count and "since 2021" tenure; verified/ID badges only if actually held.
  example_buyer_message: "You a legit seller? Don't want to get scammed."
  recommended_agent_reply: "100% fair to ask! I've got 180+ sales with a 4.9 rating, all items shipped tracked, and I disclose every flaw in the photos. Anything specific you'd like to see to feel comfortable?"
  source_quote_or_locator: "Ch.6 Authority — symbols of authority (titles, clothes, trappings) trigger deference; legitimacy can be counterfeited 'with the flimsiest of materials' (the abuse case)."
  product_stage_tags: [listing, trust, objection]
```

## 6. Buyer psychology insights
- Buyers under uncertainty default to social proof — "what are others doing?" beats your direct claims about quality. (Ch.4)
- Proof from *similar* others lands hardest; a nervous first-timer is the prime audience for "people like you bought this." (Ch.4)
- Loss looms larger than gain — buyers move faster to avoid missing out than to acquire. (Ch.7)
- Scarcity raises perceived value automatically; "rare/last one" reads as "better." (Ch.7)
- Reactance: pressure on a buyer's freedom can flip desire into refusal — being told they *can't* have it (or *must* decide now) can either inflame want or trigger a walk-away depending on framing. (Ch.7)
- A received favor creates obligation strong enough to override even dislike of the giver. (Ch.2, Regan)
- Each small commitment a buyer makes (a like, a question, a "yeah") reshapes self-image and pulls them toward the consistent next step. (Ch.3)
- Buyers defer to credible authority signals and often to mere *symbols* of authority (badges, polish, confident specifics) without scrutinizing substance. (Ch.6)
- Buyers like — and buy from — sellers who seem similar to them and who praise them, even when the motive is visible. (Ch.5)
- Buyers anchor on whatever price/quality they saw first; sequence quietly shapes their judgment. (Ch.1)

## 7. Seller psychology insights
- **Temptation to fake the trigger.** The biggest seller risk this book creates: inventing saves, sold counts, urgency, or comps because the principle "works." Cialdini's whole point is the *genuine* version works just as well — the copilot must steer the seller to real evidence only.
- **Over-pressuring** (repeated nudges, fake deadlines) feels like "closing" but triggers buyer reactance and kills deals. Sellers under-rate the freedom-restorer. (Ch.7)
- **Reflexive concession-giving** without structure trains buyers to lowball; sellers should hold a reasonable anchor and make *labeled* retreats. (Ch.2)
- **Counter-punching rude buyers** destroys liking and the sale; sellers should disengage warmly. (Ch.5)
- **Naming the favor** ("I gave you free shipping, so…") converts goodwill into resented obligation; the gift works best unspoken. (Ch.2)
- **Under-using cheap rapport** — sellers default to transactional copy and skip genuine similarity/compliments that would smooth the deal. (Ch.5)
- **Skipping the small yes** — sellers jump to "buy it?" instead of laddering a question → hold → checkout. (Ch.3)
- **Failing to confirm in writing** before sending the invoice, inviting post-deal ghosting. (Ch.3)

## 8. Ignore-list
- **Milgram obedience / electric-shock experiments** — used only as background for the authority principle; the harmful-obedience drama is irrelevant to resale.
- **Cults, religious sects (Krishna recruitment, Heaven's Gate-style), Jonestown, Tupperware-party group dynamics** — retained only as *abuse exemplars*, not as tactics to deploy.
- **Political logrolling, campaign contributions, jury/courtroom attractiveness bias** — wrong domain.
- **Boiler-room investment fraud, the "scarcity scam"** — explicitly kept as a guardrail negative example, never a play.
- **In-person-only mechanics** (cocktail-party contrast, retail floor staging, door-to-door BUG drop) — adapted to chat where portable, otherwise dropped.
- **Deep child-development / reactance-origin material (terrible twos)** — theory background, not actionable.
- **Material self-interest ("good deal") rule** — Cialdini deliberately omits it as a "goes-without-saying" given; so do we.

## 9. Guardrail flags
> Influence is the highest guardrail-risk book in the set. Every play above has a stated line it must not cross. Consolidated flags for the safety layer:

- **FAKE SCARCITY (plays 03, 04, 14):** "only one left," "others have it saved," "not restocking," "selling fast" are permitted **ONLY when literally true**. Fabricated countdowns, phantom competing buyers, or restocking a "sold out" item (the appliance-store scam, Ch.7) = hard flag. Loss-framing copy must reference a real loss.
- **FABRICATED SOCIAL PROOF (plays 01, 02):** Real sold counts, saves, likes, and reviews only. Inventing numbers, salting reviews, fake "X people bought this," or invented similar-buyer personas (salted tip jars / ringers, Ch.4) = hard flag.
- **COUNTERFEIT AUTHORITY (plays 09, 15):** Genuine receipts, serials, condition proof, and held badges only. Claiming expertise/credentials/authentication you don't have, borrowed stock photos, or fake "verified" badges (Robert-Young-as-doctor, con-man trappings, Ch.6) = hard flag.
- **MANUFACTURED OBLIGATION (plays 05, 06):** A real small gift to build goodwill is fine; **naming the debt** to extract a review/purchase, or a bad-faith inflated opening anchor whose "concession" is illusory (Ch.2 Bar-Ilan backfire) = caution/flag.
- **DARK-PATTERN PRESSURE / REACTANCE ABUSE (plays 07, 08, 14):** Friendly small-yeses and written confirmation are fine; weaponizing them to shame, trap, or relentlessly nudge a hesitant buyer, or "can't-come-back" lies (Ch.7) = flag. Default to the freedom-restorer.
- **DECEPTIVE ANCHORING (play 13):** Real comparable prices only. Phantom "compare-at" prices, struck-through prices that were never charged, or "setup" decoys (Phil's dump houses, Ch.1) = flag.
- **INSINCERE LIKING (plays 10, 11):** Genuine common ground and honest compliments only; fabricated shared interests or empty flattery as a lever (the "just like you" salesperson, Ch.5) = caution.
- **General rule (from Cialdini himself):** because each principle works as well honestly as dishonestly, the copilot should *never* need to fabricate a trigger. Any generated copy asserting a fact about demand, supply, authenticity, or comparison must be checkable against real listing/seller data before sending.

## 10. Classifiers & personas
Cialdini does not offer a formal buyer-typing model (unlike Voss). His contribution is **principle-susceptibility signals** — observable cues that tell the copilot which lever is most relevant. Kept out of `plays` per schema.

| name | signals | recommended_approach |
|---|---|---|
| Uncertain / first-time buyer | "never bought secondhand," "bit nervous," asks lots of basic questions, hesitant tone | Lead with social proof + similar-buyer proof + genuine authority/disclosure; restore freedom, don't pressure. (Ch.4, Ch.6) |
| Deal-seeker / haggler | opens with a number, "best price?", "any discount?", multiple offers | Rejection-then-retreat with a labeled concession; reciprocity gift (shipping); real-comp contrast. Hold a reasonable anchor. (Ch.2, Ch.1) |
| Fence-sitter / staller | "thinking about it," "maybe next payday," likes but doesn't buy, slow replies | One honest scarcity/loss flag, then freedom-restorer; small-yes ladder ("want me to hold it?"). Avoid reactance. (Ch.7, Ch.3) |
| Skeptic / scam-wary | "is this legit," "how do I know it's real," "don't want to get scammed" | Genuine authority: proof, serials, reviews, tenure, on-platform protection. Never bluff expertise. (Ch.6) |
| Enthusiast / fan | excited tone, knows the brand/release, "been hunting for this," emoji | Liking via similarity + sincere compliment; light scarcity ("almost kept it"). Rapport closes it. (Ch.5) |
| Independent / pressure-averse | "stop messaging," "I'll decide on my own," terse, bristles at nudges | Maximum freedom-restorer; one factual note at most. High reactance risk — back off. (Ch.7) |
| Rude / aggressive | insults, demands, hostile tone | Liking is unrecoverable via argument — stay warm/neutral, do not counter-punch, hand back control. (Ch.5, Ch.7) |
