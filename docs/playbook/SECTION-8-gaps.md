# Section 8 — What's Missing From the Sources

> Honest accounting of where the 7-book corpus runs out. The books are a deep, well-tested foundation for the *interpersonal* core of negotiation — but they were written for high-stakes, in-person, often enterprise or geopolitical contexts, not for two strangers haggling over a $30 secondhand jacket in an async chat. This section separates **(a)** what the books simply don't cover, **(b)** what *must* come from marketplace data instead, and **(c)** assumptions we are making that are currently **untested**.

---

## 8.a Gaps in the books themselves

These are things the copilot needs that **no source book provides**. They are synthesized overlays, and the product should label them as such.

1. **No scam / safety taxonomy.** This is the biggest gap. Every profile flags off-platform payment as a *safety overlay the books do not cover* (see PRIN-044 conflict_notes). The books teach the *negotiation framing* for declining ("I feel safer on-platform") but have **nothing** on the actual scam mechanics: overpayment-and-refund, fake payment screenshots, chargeback bait, ship-before-pay, code/2FA phishing, off-platform funnels. INTENT-009 and OBJ-off-platform-request are **synthesized safety overlays**, not book-derived. Critically, the books' own logic (build a golden bridge, keep the deal alive, step to their side) is *actively wrong* here — scam signals must hard-stop, never be negotiated.
2. **No shipping / chargeback / returns-logistics playbook.** Returns, prepaid labels, tracked+insured shipping, partial-refund norms, platform buyer-protection mechanics (CONC-011, PRIN-036, PRIN-043) borrow the books' *two-options-plus-fair-standard* shape, but the fair standard ("platform policy") is entirely external.
3. **No platform mechanics.** The books know nothing of likes/saves/watchers, "send offer to likers," bundle features, relist-to-refresh-feed, Best-Offer auto-decline, authenticity-guarantee programs, feed-ranking, or fee structures. Yet these are the *actual surfaces* the copilot acts on (PRIN-006, PRIN-011, PRIN-038). All platform-specific behavior is synthesized and **needs per-platform config** (Depop ≠ Vinted ≠ Poshmark ≠ eBay).
4. **No concrete numbers.** The books give *logic*, never figures. Every discount %, every "10% / 0–10% / 15–30%" in the CONC table, every photo-count or markdown-cadence threshold is a **synthesized default to be tuned**, explicitly flagged in the concession-table description. The books justify *direction* (shrinking ladder, anchor high, stay above floor) but not *magnitude*.
5. **No async-text-specific guidance.** Only *Difficult Conversations* and *Never Split the Difference* touch text/tone at all, and even DC's profile notes it had to *strip out* the in-person/video/phone material. The corpus is built on real-time, tone-rich, often face-to-face exchange. P2P resale is **async, low-context, tone-stripped, emoji-laden, mobile, often days between messages** — a channel the books barely address. Mirroring, labeling, and "go to the balcony" had to be re-grounded for text (PRIN-012, PRIN-013, PRIN-019, INTENT-014).
6. **Written for the wrong stakes/context.** The ignore_lists are revealing: Camp David, Cuban Missile Crisis, hostage negotiation, M&A, union strikes, multi-party coalitions, salary raises. These are high-stakes, high-relationship, professional-counterpart, repeated-game contexts. Casual fashion resale is **low-stakes, mostly one-shot, between non-professional strangers, over tiny dollar amounts** where the *time cost of the haggle can exceed the gap*. Whether the extracted mechanics survive that translation is itself an untested assumption (8.c).
7. **No model of the casual, conflict-averse, non-professional seller.** The books assume a motivated negotiator who wants to get good. The actual user often *hates haggling*, wants it over, and will over-cave — which is exactly why the COACH-xxx layer (especially COACH-020/PRIN-045) exists, but the seller-psychology of "I just want this gone" is more central here than in any source.

## 8.b What MUST come from marketplace data instead

The books supply *strategy*; these inputs supply the *state the strategy runs on*. None can be inferred from text alone — they require platform integration (API, scrape, or seller-provided).

1. **Behavioral signals** (flagged by the asset agent): likes / saves / watchers, days-on-market, reply-latency (buyer and seller), repeat-buyer / follower / local status, multi-item interest. These drive intent classification (INTENT-xxx signals), truthful scarcity (PRIN-011, PRIN-029 — *only* real counts), BATNA strength (PRIN-010, CONC-008), and firmness dialing (PRIN-027). **Honesty guardrail (COACH-013) makes these mandatory, not optional:** the copilot may only claim "9 people have this saved" if it can *read* that 9 is true.
2. **Comps / sold-prices.** PRIN-003 (objective criteria), PRIN-009 (anchor), and the entire concession floor depend on *real* recent-sold ranges. GTY's guardrail is explicit: never invent a sold range. If no comp feed exists, the copilot must pivot to interests/options rather than fabricate (PRIN-003 when_not_to_use).
3. **Platform rules & mechanics.** Per-platform config for: offer/counter flow, bundle support, relist behavior, fee schedule, buyer/seller protection terms, authentication programs, return policy defaults. These are the *contingent-term* and *commitment* surfaces (PRIN-025, PRIN-036, CONC-011).
4. **Category norms.** Typical price bands, discount expectations, condition-grading conventions, and seasonality differ wildly by category (sneakers vs. fast fashion vs. handbags vs. electronics). The synthesized CONC defaults are category-blind and must be specialized from data.
5. **Seller floor / BATNA.** Either seller-provided or derived from comps + days-on-market. Without it, the whole concession layer (CONC-xxx) and the trip-wire guardrail (COACH-001) have no anchor.

## 8.c Untestable assumptions to validate

We are shipping on faith on these. Each should be instrumented (§6) and tested before being trusted. Ordered roughly by risk.

1. **That book tactics transfer to casual, low-dollar, async fashion resale at all.** The corpus is high-stakes / in-person / professional / repeated-game. The central bet of this entire product is that tactical empathy, anchoring, BATNA discipline, and golden bridges still work between two strangers over a $30 item in a chat thread with 2-day reply gaps. *Plausible, but unproven in this domain.* **Test:** A/B copilot-suggested replies vs. seller-default replies on conversion, price-vs-ask, and dispute rate.
2. **That the synthesized discount defaults (CONC-xxx) are right.** "0–10% on a first lowball," "10–20% on a bundle," "15–30% partial refund" are educated guesses, not data. They could be too stingy (kills sell-through) or too generous (leaks margin) and almost certainly vary by category. **Test:** treat each threshold as a tunable; watch final-price-vs-ask and time-to-sale move together as you adjust (§5.5).
3. **That text-labeling / tactical-empathy moves land in async chat without tone.** PRIN-012 (mirror), PRIN-013 (label "it seems like…"), PRIN-016 ("that's right") were built for real-time, tone-rich speech. In a terse mobile DM, a label can read as scripted, patronizing, or robotic (the principles themselves warn of this). **Test:** seller-edit rate and conversion on label-bearing vs. plain variations; INTENT-014 ambiguous-tone handling.
4. **That the intent classifier can read intent from sparse, low-context text.** Real openers are "still avail?", "$20", "k.". INTENT-xxx assumes signals that may not be present early. **Test:** classifier precision against seller re-labels; measure misroute rate.
5. **That buyers don't perceive (and resent) the copilot.** If a haggler senses they're negotiating against an AI script, liking (PRIN-031) and rapport collapse and reactance (PRIN-033) spikes. **Test:** disclosure A/B; watch escalation and ghosting rates.
6. **That "no deal beats a bad deal" (PRIN-008/010) holds for casual sellers.** The mantra is from a high-stakes worldview. A closet-clearer's real BATNA (the item sits in a closet forever) may be *worse* than a mediocre sale — meaning the copilot should sometimes be *more* willing to deal than the books imply. **Test:** compare realized value of "held firm + relisted" vs. "took the lower offer" across aging listings.
7. **That one honesty standard fits all sellers.** COACH-013 hard-blocks fabricated scarcity/comps. Some sellers will want to bluff. We assume the ethical line is also the *profitable* line (Influence's claim that principles "work as well honestly as dishonestly"). **Test:** does honest-only framing actually match or beat bluffed framing on price *and* dispute/repeat metrics?
8. **That scam detection (synthesized, off-book) has acceptable precision/recall.** Since INTENT-009 has no source grounding, its signal list is a first guess. Both false negatives (missed scams) and false positives (legit buyers blocked) are costly. **Test:** track $-loss-to-scams (target 0) and false-positive rate (§6 #11).
